## Top News

**Super Micro Computer co-founder indicted in $2.5B AI chip smuggling scheme.** Yih-Shyan "Wally" Liaw, co-founder and SVP of Business Development at Super Micro Computer (SMCI), was indicted on charges of conspiring to violate export control laws, smuggling, and conspiracy to defraud the United States. The alleged scheme moved approximately $2.5B in Nvidia GPU servers to China through a Southeast Asian front company, with dummy servers planted at SMCI to deceive compliance officers and a U.S. export control inspector embedded at the company. Liaw was arrested and released on bail; one co-defendant is detained; a third remains at large in Taiwan. Liaw has resigned from the SMCI board and shares fell 33%, erasing more than $6B in market cap. U.S. Attorney Jay Clayton, the former SEC chair, characterized the action as a deliberate escalation of AI hardware export control enforcement. Nvidia is not implicated.

**arXiv declares independence from Cornell, to become standalone nonprofit July 1.** arXiv — the preprint server hosting foundational AI research since 1991 — announced it will separate from Cornell University and incorporate as an independent nonprofit on July 1. The move is driven by growing operating deficits ($297K in 2025), governance constraints at Cornell, and a dramatic rise in submission volume (projected 300,000+ papers this year) fueled in part by a wave of AI-generated submissions. An international CEO search is underway ($300K salary range). The Simons Foundation and Cornell are providing bridge funding. Gaining 784 points and 272 comments on Hacker News today, with community debate focused on governance of the new board structure, risk of future paywalls, and the implications of arXiv recently beginning to reject review articles — an early sign that editorial authority is already expanding ahead of independence.

**Washington State passes five AI bills as federal preemption debate heats up.** One day after the White House released its AI legislative framework calling for federal preemption of state AI laws, Washington's legislature passed five AI bills: HB 1170 (AI content disclosure), HB 2225 (chatbot safety for minors), SB 5395 (AI in health insurance decisions), SB 5105 (AI deepfakes and exploitation of minors), and SB 5886 (digital likeness rights). Washington joins Colorado and California as states with active AI legislation likely to become test cases for any federal preemption claim. No legislation implementing the White House framework has been introduced in Congress yet.

**Wall Street rebuffs Nvidia despite $1T chip forecast at GTC.** Nvidia shares slid during and after GTC 2026 (March 16–19, San Jose) despite Jensen Huang forecasting "at least" $1 trillion in cumulative Blackwell and Vera Rubin chip sales through 2027, unveiling Space-1 orbital data center GPUs, and framing the AI agent ecosystem as a $35 trillion market opportunity. Analysts at Wedbush and Wells Fargo remain bullish but attributed the sell-off to the extended timeline (through 2027, not 2026) and the absence of a surprising new product reveal. Expectations had simply been set too high to beat.

**Today's Signal:** The SMCI indictment and the GTC market reaction are different readings of the same moment: the AI hardware cycle is being stress-tested simultaneously by criminal enforcement at the supply chain and investor skepticism at the valuation layer. Washington's five-bill passage the same week the White House proposes preemption sets up a legal confrontation that could determine whether states retain any meaningful role in AI governance for the next decade.

## Funding & Launches

**Anthropic ships Claude Code Channels (v2.1.80, research preview).** Anthropic released an event-driven messaging feature for Claude Code that allows developers to push external events — CI/CD results, monitoring alerts, webhooks, and messages — into a running Claude Code session via Telegram or Discord integrations, transforming it from a terminal-only agent into a remotely accessible, event-driven one. The timing is pointed: the lack of native messaging integration was one of the primary pain points cited in Hacker News' discussion of OpenCode's breakout earlier today. Channels requires Bun runtime and claude.ai login; API key authentication is not yet supported. Gaining 395 points and 244 comments on HN.

**Atlassian cuts ~1,600 employees (10%), appoints two AI-focused co-CTOs.** The Australian enterprise software company announced a restructuring attributed to AI efficiency gains, simultaneously replacing its single CTO role with two AI-focused co-CTOs. Restructuring costs are estimated at up to $236M. The move fits a pattern emerging across this week: Block (Jack Dorsey) announced 40% staff cuts attributed to AI efficiency gains, and HSBC is reportedly weighing approximately 20,000 job cuts tied to AI-driven operational transformation.

**Today's Signal:** Claude Code Channels is a direct competitive response to the same integration pain point that made OpenCode viral this morning — and Anthropic shipped it the same day. The Atlassian restructuring alongside Block and HSBC suggests that AI-driven workforce reduction in software and financial services has moved from forecast to present tense, arriving in the same news cycle as the tools cited to justify it.

## Discussions & Debates

**FSF statement on Bartz v. Anthropic settlement asks what remedy fits.** The Free Software Foundation (FSF) published a statement on the $1.5B Bartz v. Anthropic class-action settlement — the case arising from Anthropic's training on LibGen/PiLiMi datasets — taking an unusual position: rather than seeking monetary compensation, the FSF holds copyrights on GNU (GNU's Not Unix) Project code and publications and is calling on Anthropic and other LLM developers to grant users software freedom as the appropriate remedy. The settlement's claims deadline is March 30 and a fairness hearing is set for April. HN discussion focuses on what "software freedom as compensation" would even mean in practice — open-sourcing weights? Publishing training data? No clean mechanism exists, and the FSF's framing implicitly challenges whether the copyright liability framework is the right lens for AI training disputes at all.

**Senior European journalist suspended for AI-fabricated quotes — while writing about AI ethics.** Peter Vandermeersch, former CEO of Mediahuis Ireland and a 9-year editor-in-chief at NRC, was suspended after an NRC investigation found 15 of 53 posts in his newsletter contained quotes that did not appear in cited sources, with seven individuals confirming they never said the things attributed to them. He used ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Google NotebookLM to summarize reports without verifying AI-generated attribution. In his admission he wrote that "these language models produce irresistible quotes you are tempted to use as an author." The particular irony: his newsletter covered journalism practice, including pieces arguing AI companies used copyrighted content without consent. HN discussion centers on professional accountability and whether systemic economic pressures on journalism are accelerating these failures across the industry.

**Today's Signal:** Both debates are about what happens when AI systems produce outputs indistinguishable from authentic ones — a fabricated quote, a training corpus assembled without consent, a policy claim with no enforcement mechanism. The FSF's provocation is structural: if money is a poor remedy for AI training harms, the alternative remedies don't yet exist. The Vandermeersch case is the sharpest failure narrative of the day — a knowledgeable, senior person was systematically deceived by tools he understood well enough to critique publicly.
