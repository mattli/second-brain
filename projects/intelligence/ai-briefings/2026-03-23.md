# AI Intelligence Briefing — 2026-03-23

## Top News

*Cursor admits Composer 2 was built on Chinese open-source model Kimi K2.5 — without disclosing it at launch.* The well-funded US AI coding startup ($2.3B raised, reportedly $2B+ annualized revenue) quietly released its new flagship coding model on March 19, describing it only as "frontier-level coding intelligence." An X user discovered Kimi code signatures inside the product; Cursor VP Lee Robinson confirmed the Kimi K2.5 foundation and co-founder Aman Sanger called the omission "a miss." Cursor says roughly 25% of the model's total compute came from the Kimi base with ~75% from its own training, and that the use was licensed under an authorized commercial partnership with Moonshot AI through Fireworks AI. On its internal CursorBench, Composer 2 scores 61.3 — above Claude Opus 4.6 (58.2) but below GPT-5.4 Thinking (63.9), at $0.50/$2.50 per million tokens.

*Musk announces Terafab — a $25B chip fab in Austin under Tesla, SpaceX, and xAI.* At the defunct Seaholm Power Plant in Austin before Texas Governor Greg Abbott, Musk announced plans to build what he called "the most epic chip-building exercise in history by far" — a semiconductor fabrication facility targeting one terawatt of annual compute, integrated with Giga Texas. The facility would produce two chip families: one for automotive and robotics edge inference (Tesla, Optimus), and one hardened for space-based AI compute aboard SpaceX satellites. Musk acknowledged continued dependence on TSMC, Samsung, and Micron. No construction timeline was provided, and the $25B estimated cost is not currently in Tesla's 2026 capex plan.

*Walmart releases first hard data on agentic commerce: ChatGPT Instant Checkout converted at one-third the rate of its website.* Walmart's EVP of product Daniel Danker disclosed that the 200,000-product integration launched in November — allowing purchases directly inside ChatGPT — produced conversion rates 3× lower than click-throughs to Walmart.com. The core failure: single-item-only transactions and no cart bundling. Walmart is pivoting to embed its own chatbot Sparky inside ChatGPT and Google Gemini, retaining customer data, cart logic, and transaction ownership while using OpenAI as a discovery surface.

**Today's Signal:** All three stories are different versions of the same stress test: AI capabilities are being productized faster than the norms around disclosure, feasibility, and user experience can keep up. Cursor shipped a China-origin model without provenance disclosure; Musk announced a chip factory he cannot yet build on a timeline he hasn't set; Walmart's direct data confirms that replacing optimized e-commerce UX with conversational AI does not automatically improve outcomes. The recurring pattern is announcement-to-reality gap.

## Funding & Launches

*Flash-MoE: Running Qwen 3.5 397B on a MacBook at 5.5 tokens/second.* An open-source project by developer Dan Veloper (GitHub: danveloper/flash-moe) implements Apple's 2023 "LLM in a Flash" research paper to stream Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) model weights from an SSD through custom Metal GPU shaders, keeping only the 4 active expert modules (out of 512 per layer) in memory at any time. The 209GB model fits in 48GB unified memory thanks to 2-bit quantization of expert weights, with non-expert matrices held at full precision. Gaining 365 points on Hacker News today. The developer built all the C/Metal code in roughly 24 hours using Claude Code to run automated experiments.

**Today's Signal:** Flash-MoE is a technique story more than a product story — the practical throughput (4–5 tokens/sec) limits real-world use, and HN discussion notes quality degradation at 2-bit quantization. Its significance is as a proof of concept: Apple Silicon's SSD bandwidth plus MoE sparsity can substitute for GPU VRAM in ways that were theoretical until now. For builders evaluating edge and offline deployments, this shifts what's architecturally possible on consumer hardware.

## Discussions & Debates

*"Reports of code's death are greatly exaggerated"* — A 465-point Hacker News thread on an essay by Steve Krouse (stevekrouse.com/precision) arguing that AI coding tools are a productivity multiplier, not a replacement for human programming judgment. The core claim: AI "accepts conventional wisdom" and cannot generate genuinely innovative code — citing Chris Lattner's finding that AI compiler output contained nothing architecturally novel. The essay specifically targets "vibe coding," arguing it "gives the illusion that your vibes are precise abstractions" until lower-level bugs surface and "wreck your whole day." HN commenters largely agree on the practical limits but debate whether innovation is the right metric, noting that most human-written code lacks innovation too.

*"Migrating to the EU"* — A 414-point Hacker News thread on a guide for replacing US tech services with European alternatives (rz01.org/eu-migration/). The discussion is driven by a mix of data-sovereignty concerns and political discontent with US tech incumbents: Gmail to Proton Mail, Google Drive to Infomaniak, Google Maps to TomTom. Notably motivated by Canadians and Europeans — not just EU residents — signaling that concerns about US tech control extend beyond regulatory compliance.

**Today's Signal:** Both threads reflect the same underlying question about who controls the software stack — and whether dependence on US AI incumbents is a technical choice or a political one. The Cursor provenance story and the EU migration thread both point to a growing builder anxiety about supply chain transparency: where does the code come from, and who controls it? This is a different kind of concern than capability gaps, and it's gaining surface area.
