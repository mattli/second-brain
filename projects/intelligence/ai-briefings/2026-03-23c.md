# AI Intelligence Briefing — 2026-03-23

## Top News

**OpenAI courts private equity with 17.5% guaranteed return amid Anthropic enterprise gains.** Reuters reports (exclusive, today) that OpenAI is offering PE firms a guaranteed 17.5% return to attract capital for joint ventures designed to ease enterprises' high upfront AI deployment costs. Some PE firms are skeptical about the joint venture profit profile. The move comes as Anthropic has reportedly seized roughly 73% of first-time enterprise AI spending — up from a 50/50 split with OpenAI just ten weeks ago, per Ramp's spending data.

**Walmart: ChatGPT in-chat checkout converted 3x worse than its website.** Walmart tested roughly 200,000 products through OpenAI's "Instant Checkout" feature (which let users buy inside ChatGPT without visiting Walmart.com) from November onward. Walmart's EVP of product and design called the in-chat experience "unsatisfying" — in-chat purchases converted at one-third the rate of standard click-out transactions. OpenAI has since confirmed it is phasing out Instant Checkout in favor of merchant-handled checkout. The story is generating significant discussion today after surfacing on Hacker News.

**ECB: AI could boost euro area productivity by 4% over the next decade.** In a speech today at an ECB-SAFE research conference, ECB Executive Board member Philip Lane presented staff estimates projecting a roughly 4% cumulative productivity gain for the euro area over ten years from AI diffusion — covering investment, productivity, and employment effects.

**Google president at CERAWeek: the US needs more energy infrastructure to power AI.** At the CERAWeek (Cambridge Energy Research Associates) energy industry conference today, Google's president called for additional US energy development capacity, framing power access as a binding constraint on AI expansion.

**Today's Signal:** The Walmart data lands at a pivotal moment — it's the clearest hard evidence yet that agentic commerce inside LLM chat interfaces underperforms traditional web commerce, and OpenAI has already responded by pulling Instant Checkout. Combined with OpenAI's PE pitch amid an Anthropic enterprise surge, the pattern is one of competitive pressure translating into unusual financial engineering. The real bottleneck may not be model capability but infrastructure and incentive alignment.

## Funding & Launches

**Nebius closes $4.34 billion in convertible debt financing** (Reuters, today). The AI cloud infrastructure company — spun out of Russian search giant Yandex — says it is now "well-funded" for the AI race and plans to grow using a mix of customer prepayments and equity-debt capital. Nebius is positioning AI cloud services as its primary growth driver.

**Gimlet Labs raises $80 million Series A** for chip-agnostic AI inference (TechCrunch, today). Gimlet's technology lets AI workloads run simultaneously across NVIDIA, AMD, Intel, ARM, Cerebras, and d-Matrix chips — targeting major model labs and large data centers, not typical app developers. The company launched publicly in October with eight-figure revenues and says its customer base has more than doubled in four months.

**HSBC appoints its first chief AI officer** (Reuters, today), as the bank signals a formal push to use AI for cost reduction.

**Today's Signal:** Both the Nebius and Gimlet raises point at infrastructure rather than applications — one bets on AI cloud services, the other on solving hardware fragmentation at the inference layer. Capital is flowing toward the picks-and-shovels layer, not the model layer, suggesting investors see the infrastructure buildout as still early and the hardware monoculture around NVIDIA as a risk worth betting against.

## Discussions & Debates

**"If DSPy is so great, why isn't anyone using it?"** — A blog post by Skylar Payne (published March 21, gaining traction on HN today) examines why DSPy — a framework for algorithmically optimizing LLM (large language model) prompts — has 4.7 million monthly downloads while LangChain has 222 million despite DSPy arguably being better designed. The core argument: DSPy forces good software engineering abstractions upfront (typed I/O, composable modules, separated optimizers), but engineers under pressure to ship avoid the unfamiliar learning curve. The result is that most teams end up building a "worse version of DSPy" anyway, after months of pain. Payne coins a "Khattab's Law" (a riff on Greenspun's Tenth Rule): *any sufficiently complicated AI system contains an ad hoc, informally-specified, bug-ridden implementation of half of DSPy.*

**WSJ: "What young workers are doing to AI-proof themselves"** is drawing 195 points and 300+ comments on HN today. The thread surfaces anxiety about which skills remain durable as AI takes over more junior work — with HN commenters debating whether the right response is "go deeper on judgment-heavy work," "specialize in areas models get wrong," or simply "accept that the floor has risen and compete on output, not hours."

**Today's Signal:** Both threads reveal the same underlying anxiety from different angles — practitioners struggling to know which abstractions are worth building on versus which are temporary scaffolding. The DSPy debate is really about the maturity of AI engineering as a discipline; the career-proofing debate is about what AI engineering does to the workforce beneath it.
