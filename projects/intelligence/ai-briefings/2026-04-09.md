# AI Intelligence Briefing — 2026-04-09

## Top News

**OpenAI projects $2.5B in ad revenue this year, $100B by 2030.** Axios reported Thursday that OpenAI has told investors its advertising business will generate $2.5 billion in 2026 and reach $100 billion by 2030, assuming ChatGPT reaches 2.75 billion weekly users. The ad pilot — launched in January to U.S. users on the free and Go tiers — crossed $100 million in annualized recurring revenue in under two months with more than 600 advertisers. Projected trajectory: $2.5B (2026) → $11B (2027) → $25B (2028) → $53B (2029) → $100B (2030). OpenAI frames advertising as a path to funding AI at scale for users who cannot afford subscriptions. The move sharpens an explicit competitive split: Anthropic ran a Super Bowl commercial this year positioning Claude as permanently ad-free, while OpenAI CEO Sam Altman has said ads allow the product to reach more people. Separately, CFO Sarah Friar confirmed on April 8 that OpenAI will allocate IPO shares to retail investors ahead of its planned public debut.

**Amazon's annual shareholder letter reveals AWS AI revenue at a $15B annualized run rate; custom chips at $20B.** In the first public disclosure of Amazon's AI revenue figures, CEO Andy Jassy's shareholder letter — published Thursday — revealed AWS AI services generated an annualized revenue run rate above $15 billion in Q1 2026, roughly 10% of AWS's total $142B run rate. Amazon's custom chips business (Graviton processors, Trainium AI chips, Nitro networking cards) doubled its annualized run rate to $20 billion, up from the $10 billion disclosed at Q4 earnings. Jassy defended Amazon's $200B capex plan for 2026 — "We're not investing...on a hunch" — citing committed customer contracts for a substantial portion of the spending, with most monetization flowing in 2027–2028. He also said Amazon may sell Trainium chip racks to third parties in the future, following Google's TPU playbook.

**D.C. appeals court declines to block Pentagon's Anthropic blacklisting; small AI rivals report a surge of demand.** A Washington D.C. federal appeals court on April 8 declined to temporarily block the Pentagon's supply-chain risk designation of Anthropic — a result that contradicts an earlier California court ruling that had temporarily blocked a narrower version of the same designation. The D.C. case concerns a law that could trigger a government-wide civilian blacklist through an interagency review process; the California case covers the narrower Pentagon-specific statute. In parallel, Reuters reported Thursday that small defense-focused AI startups — including Smack Technologies and EdgeRunner AI — are receiving surges of contract discussions, military meeting requests, and investor inquiries in the wake of Anthropic's ouster. EdgeRunner AI's CEO said a Space Force contract that had been stalled for more than a year was signed within weeks of the Anthropic situation going public.

**Meta and CoreWeave expand AI cloud deal to $21B through 2032.** CoreWeave and Meta Platforms announced Thursday a new $21 billion agreement to supply AI cloud capacity through December 2032, including some of the first deployments of Nvidia's next-generation Vera Rubin platform. The new deal runs on top of an existing $14.2 billion agreement signed in September 2025. CoreWeave CEO Mike Intrator said the focus of the expanded capacity is inference — running Meta's AI systems continuously at scale rather than training new ones — and that no single customer will now represent more than 35% of total revenue, down from Microsoft's 62% share in 2024. The announcement came the day after Meta debuted its Muse Spark AI model (covered in Notable Model Releases below). Meta has projected $115–135B in AI-related capital expenditures for 2026. CoreWeave shares rose approximately 4–8% on the news.

**SiFive raises $400M from Atreides and Nvidia for RISC-V data center CPUs.** Silicon Valley chip IP startup SiFive announced a $400 million funding round led by Atreides Management with Nvidia as an investor, at a $3.65 billion valuation. CEO Patrick Little told Reuters it is likely the company's last private round before filing for an IPO. SiFive sells chip design blueprints — not chips — based on RISC-V, an open chip standard not controlled by any single company. The timing targets a specific opportunity: Arm Holdings unveiled its own chips in March, making Arm for the first time a competitor to many of its longtime licensees (including Google). SiFive plans to use the funding to build a RISC-V data center CPU design, entering the market directly as Arm, Nvidia, and Intel all converge on the same space.

**Today's Signal:** The throughline in today's news is AI monetization becoming real and measurable across the board. OpenAI disclosing $2.5B in projected ad revenue, Amazon disclosing $15B in actual AI cloud revenue, Meta locking in $21B in inference infrastructure commitments — these are Q1 2026 run rates, not projections about a future market. The Anthropic/Pentagon story is the shadow side of the same theme: the companies now winning government AI contracts (OpenAI, xAI) are the ones that chose contract access over principled usage restrictions. The OpenAI ad revenue disclosure and the Anthropic court loss arrive in the same news cycle, marking a clean fork between two different theories of how to build a large AI company.

## Notable Model Releases

**Meta debuts Muse Spark, the first model from Meta Superintelligence Labs.** Announced April 8, Muse Spark is the first major model since Alexandr Wang joined Meta as Chief AI Officer nine months ago as part of Meta's $14.3 billion Scale AI acquisition. The model is deliberately small and fast rather than frontier-scale: Meta says it achieves "competitive performance in multimodal perception, reasoning, health, and agentic tasks" and was built for "an order of magnitude less compute" than Meta's older midsize Llama 4 variant. Three modes are included: a fast mode for simple queries, a standard mode for complex tasks (legal documents, nutritional analysis from photos), and a "Contemplating" mode that deploys a squad of parallel AI agents to "compete with the extreme reasoning modes of frontier models such as Gemini Deep Think and GPT Pro." Crucially, Muse Spark is proprietary — Meta "hopes to open-source future versions" but is breaking from the Llama open-source strategy for this model family. A paid API for third-party developers is planned; only "select partners" have private preview access. The model now powers the Meta AI app and website and will roll out to Facebook, Instagram, WhatsApp, Messenger, and Ray-Ban Meta glasses in the coming weeks. Meta also plans a Shopping mode that draws on "styling inspiration and brand storytelling" from its social platforms.

**Today's Signal:** Muse Spark is a course correction, not a milestone. Meta's Llama 4 family failed to generate significant developer adoption; this model is proprietary, agentic, and explicitly positioned against frontier reasoning models. Wang's stated gap areas — "long-horizon agentic systems and coding workflows" — are exactly where OpenAI and Anthropic are strongest. The pivot from open-source to proprietary signals that Meta believes differentiated performance, not openness, is what wins the model market in 2026 — and that after spending $14.3B on Wang, the company needs commercial returns, not developer goodwill.

## Funding & Launches

**Intel and Google expand AI CPU partnership, driven by agentic AI demand.** The companies announced Thursday an expanded agreement under which Google will continue deploying Intel's Xeon and Xeon 6 processors for inference and general-purpose workloads, and will co-develop custom infrastructure processing units (IPUs) — chips that can handle tasks traditionally managed by the CPU, enabling more efficient compute. Intel CEO Lip-Bu Tan: "Scaling AI requires more than accelerators — it requires balanced systems." The underlying driver is agentic AI: systems that perform multi-step autonomous operations require significantly more CPU capacity than single-turn request/response AI. Intel this week also announced it is joining Musk's Terafab AI chip complex project with SpaceX and Tesla.

**AfterQuery raises $30M Series A at $300M valuation** for AI-powered data analytics. The San Francisco-based startup, which enables businesses to query their data through natural language, reached a $300 million valuation on the raise.

**Today's Signal:** Intel's expanded Google partnership — plus entry into the Terafab project — is the most commercially significant Intel AI story since the company lost the early AI GPU race to Nvidia. The CPU-revival thesis is real: agentic workloads don't run on GPUs alone, and Intel holds a large share of the existing server CPU install base. AfterQuery's raise reflects steady Series A activity in the data-to-AI analytics category as enterprises move from AI pilots to integrating AI into their data stacks.

## Discussions & Debates

**"Walmart: ChatGPT checkout converted 3x worse than website" resurfaces on Hacker News (241 points, 176 comments).** A Search Engine Land article from March 19 — reporting that Walmart's EVP of product and design Daniel Danker publicly stated that in-ChatGPT Instant Checkout purchases converted at one-third the rate of traditional website click-outs, calling the experience "unsatisfying" — is trending on HN today, made newly topical by OpenAI's ad revenue projections. Starting in November, Walmart offered roughly 200,000 products through OpenAI's Instant Checkout feature, allowing purchases to be completed inside ChatGPT without visiting Walmart's site; Danker confirmed Walmart is moving away from it. OpenAI has since phased out Instant Checkout in favor of merchant-handled app checkout. The HN thread debates whether the data shows a structural failure of AI-native commerce UX, or whether Instant Checkout was simply poorly implemented.

**"Reports of code's death are greatly exaggerated" by Steve Krouse (Val Town founder) is trending on Hacker News (507 points, 364 comments).** The essay, written in March and gaining HN traction today, argues that vibe coding — using AI to generate code from natural language descriptions — creates an illusion of precision that fails when real complexity arrives. Krouse's core argument: English specifications "intuitively feel precise until you learn better from bitter experience," and vibe coding delays rather than eliminates the reckoning with abstraction. The deeper claim is that the irreplaceable programmer skill is not syntax mastery but the ability to compress complex systems into clean conceptual layers — an activity that gets *harder*, not easier, as codebase complexity grows. His closing analogy: "Nobody is out there claiming that ChatGPT is putting the great novelists or journalists out of jobs." The HN thread splits between those who see this as accurate and necessary pushback on AI-hype overreach, and those who argue Krouse underestimates how fast the abstraction gap is closing.

**Today's Signal:** Both threads are running measurements against the same overextended narrative — that AI already does what humans do. The Walmart conversion data is a clean controlled experiment: AI-native checkout, on real transactions at scale, at a major retailer, underperformed traditional UX by a factor of three. The Krouse essay applies the same logic to programming: current AI tools remove syntactic friction without removing the conceptually hard part. What's notable is that both calibrations are happening in practitioner communities — developers and commerce engineers — not in policy or press contexts. The practitioners are updating their models on actual data, which is a different and slower signal than the capabilities announcements that have dominated the news cycle.
