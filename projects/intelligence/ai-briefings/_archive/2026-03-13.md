# AI Intelligence Briefing — 2026-03-13

## Executive Summary

Meta's flagship AI model, codenamed Avocado, has been delayed to May and is reportedly falling short of newer offerings from OpenAI, Google, and Anthropic — with Meta leadership actively discussing licensing Google's Gemini as a temporary stop-gap, a remarkable signal of competitive pressure on the world's largest social platform. Elsewhere today, the Anthropic-versus-Pentagon standoff deepened as employees from OpenAI and Google publicly backed Anthropic's lawsuit after the Department of Defense designated the company a "supply-chain risk" for refusing to enable mass surveillance or autonomous weapons — an unusual show of cross-industry solidarity. A wave of state AI legislation passed today across Washington, Utah, and Virginia, while a consumer trust survey found that despite 60% weekly AI usage, only 13% of people fully trust it. The day's open question: as Meta scrambles and AI governance proliferates, which lab — or which coalition — will define the trust standards that let AI move from tool to infrastructure?

## Top News

- *Meta delays "Avocado" AI to May.* According to a New York Times report cited by Fortune (March 13), Meta's latest model underperforms Google Gemini 3.0 and recent offerings from OpenAI and Anthropic despite $14.3B in investment. Meta leadership has discussed licensing Gemini from Google as an interim solution — a striking admission from a company that also competes with Google in ads, video, and hardware.

- *Perplexity launches "Personal Computer."* Perplexity introduced a local deployment of its Computer agent platform that runs continuously on a dedicated Mac mini, positioning the product as a privacy-conscious alternative to cloud-based agent platforms. It marks a notable strategic shift from search and Q&A toward persistent, local-first AI agents.

- *AI legislation passes in three states.* Washington passed HB 1170 (AI disclosure) and HB 2225 (chatbot safety for minors), Utah passed its AI bills, and Virginia passed three AI measures — all on the March 13 adjournment deadline. Florida's AI Bill of Rights passed the Senate 35-2 but appears stalled in the House as the legislature closes out today.

- *X restricts Grok to paid subscribers.* X (formerly Twitter) moved "Ask Grok" behind its Premium paywall, ending free access to the AI feature. The move has renewed debate about whether AI assistance in social platforms should be treated as a utility or a premium service.

**Today's Signal:** Meta's stumble with Avocado — combined with Perplexity pivoting toward persistent local agents and X monetizing AI access — suggests the frontier model race is splintering into differentiated bets: scale, privacy, and platform lock-in. The companies that can't win on raw capability are now racing to win on positioning.

Knuth called it "a dramatic advance in automatic deduction and creative problem solving."

**Today's Signal:** The absence of major releases today is less a pause than a reloading. The accelerating cadence of the past several weeks — with 12+ models in one week in early March — suggests the next wave is close. Meta's delay is the exception that proves the rule: even well-resourced labs are now feeling the pace of the frontier.

## What Builders Are Talking About

- *OpenAI Codex scans 1.2M commits, finds 10,561 high-severity bugs.* OpenAI released a Codex Security agent that scanned open-source commits and surfaced 10,561 high-severity vulnerabilities, triggering active discussion on Hacker News about AI-assisted security auditing at scale — and what it means that AI now finds more bugs than human reviewers.

- *Anthropic Claude finds 22 Firefox vulnerabilities.* In a disclosed security partnership with Mozilla, Claude Opus 4.6 identified 22 new browser vulnerabilities — 14 high-severity — addressed in Firefox 148. Builders are noting that the number of high-severity bugs Claude found represents nearly a fifth of all such bugs patched in Firefox in all of 2025, fueling discussion about AI as a first-pass security auditor.

- *Claude Code vs. Codex debate intensifies on Reddit.* Community analysis across r/ClaudeCode and r/ChatGPTCoding finds Claude Code wins blind code-quality tests at a 67% rate but hits usage limits too quickly for daily use, while Codex is rated slightly lower quality but more reliable for sustained workflows. The emerging consensus: use both. OpenClaw — an open-source local AI assistant gateway with 210K+ GitHub stars — continues to see rapid growth as a privacy-preserving alternative stack.

**Today's Signal:** Security is emerging as a high-signal proving ground for AI capability — and for trust. The builder community is watching closely to see whether AI-generated code introduces as many vulnerabilities as AI security tools find. That gap, not benchmarks, may be the real measure of readiness for production use.

## Funding & Launches

- *Nvidia invests $2B in Nebius, taking an 8.3% stake.* Announced March 11, the deal commits Nvidia to co-building gigawatt-scale AI data centers with the Amsterdam-based neocloud (formerly Yandex). Nebius stock jumped 16%. Nvidia has now made similar $2B strategic investments in CoreWeave, Synopsys, Lumentum, and Coherent in quick succession — effectively using investment to lock in the next layer of the AI stack.

- *Meta acquires Moltbook.* Meta bought Moltbook, an AI agent social network that hit millions of registered bots within days of launch, built on OpenClaw's multi-agent infrastructure. The team joins Meta's superintelligence labs. OpenAI had already hired Moltbook's founder in a prior move.

- *Microsoft Copilot Cowork rolling out late March.* The agentic productivity product, powered by Anthropic's Claude underneath, lets users describe outcomes and have Copilot work across Outlook, Teams, and Excel to execute them. Commercial rollout begins this month.

**Today's Signal:** Capital is concentrating on AI infrastructure and agents — not just models. Nvidia's series of $2B ecosystem investments and Meta's Moltbook acquisition both point toward a world where the real competitive moat is the layer between models and users: the networks, agents, and infrastructure that make AI persistent rather than reactive.

## Discussions & Debates

- *Anthropic-DOD lawsuit draws cross-company solidarity.* More than 30 employees from OpenAI and Google DeepMind signed a public statement backing Anthropic's lawsuit after the Pentagon designated the company a "supply-chain risk" — typically reserved for foreign adversaries — after Anthropic refused to allow its technology to be used for mass surveillance or autonomous weapons firing. The DOD then quickly signed a deal with OpenAI, which prompted internal protest within OpenAI itself. The debate on Hacker News and X centers on whether "supply-chain risk" is being weaponized as regulatory pressure, and what it means for AI companies to take public positions on military use.

- *"Einstein" bot sharpens classroom AI debate.* A Nature article published March 12 highlights how faculty are spending increasing time detecting and outmaneuvering student AI use, with one case involving a chatbot impersonating Einstein to deliver course material. The piece has reignited a debate about whether institutions should be resisting, regulating, or redesigning around AI — with no consensus emerging.

**Today's Signal:** The AI community's anxiety is splitting along two fault lines: governance (who controls how AI is used by governments and institutions?) and trust (can AI-generated work be verified?). Both questions are moving faster than any policy or cultural framework can keep up with.
