# AI Intelligence Briefing — 2026-04-01

## Top News

**Anthropic leaks Claude Code source code in second security lapse within a week.** Anthropic confirmed that it accidentally exposed approximately 500,000 lines of Claude Code source code across roughly 1,900 TypeScript files when it uploaded the uncompiled source to npm instead of the finished compiled package. Version 2.1.88 of the `@anthropic-ai/claude-code` npm package was pulled after users spotted an included source map file that allowed extraction of the full codebase. Anthropic said no customer data or credentials were exposed, attributing the incident to "human error." The leak is potentially more damaging than last week's Mythos blog post exposure: competitors could reverse-engineer Claude Code's agentic harness architecture and developers may use the leaked code to build open-source alternatives. The back-to-back disclosures — both caused by human error in release processes — are generating significant reputational friction for a company whose core public identity is responsible, deliberate development.

**Salesforce reinvents Slack as an agentic AI assistant, taking direct aim at Microsoft Copilot.** In a keynote address in San Francisco, Salesforce CEO Marc Benioff unveiled a major overhaul of Slack that transforms the built-in Slackbot into a full agentic assistant powered by Anthropic. The revamped Slackbot can query Salesforce CRM data in natural language, adjust records, select from specialized "skills," and be detached from the Slack interface entirely to run as a desktop assistant. The positioning is explicit: this is Salesforce's counter to Microsoft Copilot. Salesforce stock is down roughly 30% this year amid fears that AI will eat SaaS businesses — the Slack refresh is Benioff's most concrete argument that AI makes Salesforce products more valuable rather than obsolete.

**OpenAI raises additional $12 billion including from retail investors.** OpenAI announced it has raised an additional $12 billion as a tranche of the $110 billion round announced in February, bringing that round's total to $122 billion. The post-money valuation is now $852 billion. Notably, $3 billion of the new capital comes from retail investors — wealthy individuals who were offered the opportunity to buy in through their banks — and OpenAI stock will be included in several ARK Invest exchange-traded funds. OpenAI framed the retail component as "giving more people the opportunity to share in the upside economics of the AI era." It also exposes them to the downside risk, which OpenAI did not emphasize.

**Walmart shuts down ChatGPT Instant Checkout after internal data shows 3x worse conversions.** Walmart has pulled back from OpenAI's Instant Checkout feature inside ChatGPT after internal data showed conversion rates were approximately three times lower than transactions completed on Walmart's own website, as first reported by Wired and now gaining significant traction on Hacker News. OpenAI is reportedly reworking the commerce model as retailers push to retain control over the checkout flow and customer data. Walmart's new approach is to embed its own AI assistant, Sparky, into ChatGPT while keeping the cart and transaction layer on its own platform — a model the company says is already showing stronger performance.

**Today's Signal:** The Anthropic double disclosure — Mythos blog last week, source code today — and the Walmart conversion data land on the same day to underscore two different kinds of AI credibility gap: one about operational reliability at the company building the most safety-conscious AI, and one about whether AI-native product interfaces actually convert users into customers. Salesforce's Slack bet is the most concrete counter-narrative: that AI augments existing software rather than replacing it. OpenAI's retail investor move is a new kind of signal — the company is not just raising capital at a $852B valuation, it is socializing that valuation into index funds and individual portfolios, which has implications for how a future IPO is received.

## Funding & Launches

**Microsoft and Chevron plan $7 billion natural gas power plant in Texas.** Bloomberg reported that Microsoft and Chevron are in talks with Engine No. 1 to build a 2,500-megawatt natural gas power facility in Texas to supply AI data center demand. The deal, if completed, would be one of the largest single power investments tied directly to AI compute infrastructure. It follows Microsoft's earlier nuclear energy agreements and signals that hyperscalers are moving from power purchasing agreements to direct power plant ownership to secure long-term AI compute capacity.

**Oracle cutting thousands of employees amid AI infrastructure spending.** CNBC reported that Oracle is conducting a significant layoff affecting thousands of employees — against a backdrop of the company simultaneously investing heavily in AI data center infrastructure and GPU capacity. Oracle had approximately 162,000 employees as of mid-2025. The pattern — aggressive infrastructure investment alongside headcount reduction — mirrors the broader enterprise AI moment: capital spending and labor spending are moving in opposite directions.

**Today's Signal:** The Microsoft/Chevron power plant and Oracle's workforce contraction tell the same infrastructure story from two angles: AI's energy and compute requirements have reached a scale where hyperscalers are becoming utilities, and that same investment pressure is reshaping corporate headcount at companies that can't afford both. The transition from software companies to infrastructure operators is accelerating.

## Discussions & Debates

**"Walmart: ChatGPT checkout converted 3x worse than website" lands on HN front page (241 points, 176 comments).** The HN discussion around the Walmart data is generating substantive pushback against the AI-native commerce thesis. Key threads argue that AI checkout UX removes the product discovery and impulse-purchase layers that traditional e-commerce interfaces are optimized for; that users in a ChatGPT interface have different intent than users on a retailer's own site; and that the conversion gap may reflect the mismatch between "AI as assistant" and "AI as storefront" rather than a fundamental failure of the technology. Several comments note that Walmart's pivot — keeping AI as a recommendation layer while retaining the transactional interface — is the correct architectural response. Others argue the data is an early signal that the "AI replaces the website" thesis applied to commerce is simply wrong.

**"If DSpy is so great, why isn't anyone using it?" surfaces on HN (69 points, 46 comments).** A post examining the gap between DSPy's (Declarative Self-improving Python, a framework for optimizing LLM pipelines) academic reputation and its real-world adoption is generating candid discussion about ML tooling. The central argument: DSPy's programming model — where prompts are replaced with declarative modules optimized by the framework — requires a shift in how developers think about LLM applications that most teams are not willing to make. Discussion threads surface a recurring pattern across AI tooling: frameworks that require rearchitecting application logic rarely win against incumbents that improve iteratively on existing mental models, regardless of their theoretical advantages.

**Today's Signal:** Both threads converge on the same structural question the field keeps re-encountering: what kind of AI product actually fits into existing user behavior and developer workflows, versus what requires users and builders to change how they work? Walmart's conversion data and DSPy's adoption gap are both answers to the same question — the harder the behavioral change required, the slower the uptake, regardless of the capability improvement on offer.
