## Top News

**New court filings in Anthropic-Pentagon case contradict public statements.** Anthropic submitted two sworn declarations to a California federal court ahead of Tuesday's March 24 hearing. Anthropic's Head of Policy Sarah Heck — who was present at the February 24 meeting between CEO Dario Amodei and Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth — stated that Anthropic never claimed it wanted an "approval role" over military operations, calling that claim "simply not true." More significantly, she documented that on March 4, the day after the Pentagon formally finalized its supply-chain risk designation against Anthropic, Under Secretary Emil Michael emailed Amodei saying the two sides were "very close" on the issues cited as threats. Two days later, Michael posted publicly that there was "no active negotiation"; a week after that, he told CNBC there was "no chance" of renewed talks. Anthropic's Head of Public Sector also denied the existence of any remote "kill switch," stating the company cannot access DoD systems and can only update Claude with government and AWS approval.

**Microsoft rolls back Copilot AI bloat in Windows.** After sustained user and enterprise backlash against forced AI integration, Microsoft announced it is reducing Copilot entry points across Windows — removing integrations from Photos, Widgets, Notepad, and Snipping Tool, and shelving planned integrations in Settings, notifications, and File Explorer. The company also halted automatic installation of the Microsoft 365 Copilot app on enterprise machines. EVP Pavan Davuluri framed the change as becoming "more intentional about how and where Copilot integrates," focusing on AI "where it's genuinely useful" — a significant rhetorical retreat from the company's 2024–2025 "AI everywhere" posture.

**OpenCode breaks out on Hacker News with 1,000+ points.** The open-source AI coding agent built by Anomaly Innovations hit the top of Hacker News today with over 1,000 points and 524 comments — the largest single-day engagement for an AI coding tool on the platform in recent memory. OpenCode supports 75+ models including Claude, GPT-5, Gemini, and local models via Ollama, offering a provider-agnostic alternative to Claude Code and GitHub Copilot. The project has 126,000 GitHub stars and 5 million monthly active users. Today's discussion centered on its privacy-first architecture, multi-session support, and compatibility with the Agent Client Protocol (ACP) standard for IDE integration.

**Meta's Omnilingual MT brings machine translation to 1,600 languages.** Meta's FAIR (Fundamental AI Research) lab published research on Omnilingual MT, a suite of models achieving state-of-the-art machine translation across 1,600 languages — up from roughly 200 in its previous No Language Left Behind system. Remarkably, a 3B parameter model matches or exceeds translation quality of 70B LLM baselines across this range, with meaningful output spanning approximately 1,200 languages on the target side. The paper is on arXiv (2603.16309) and the evaluation leaderboard and datasets are freely available. The result gains HN traction today with 69 points.

**Today's Signal:** The Copilot rollback and the OpenCode surge are mirror images: one is an incumbent retreating from forced AI integration, the other is an open-source project succeeding precisely because it doesn't force anything. The Anthropic-Pentagon case is the highest-stakes version of the same tension — institutions making AI decisions unilaterally, then managing the credibility problem when the gap between public statements and internal communications becomes visible.

## Notable Model Releases

**Mamba-3 (CMU / Princeton / Together AI / Cartesia AI — released March 17, accepted at ICLR 2026).** A new State Space Model (SSM) designed around inference efficiency rather than training speed — a deliberate inversion of Mamba-2's priorities. Core innovations include exponential-trapezoidal discretization, complex-valued state updates, and a Multi-Input Multi-Output (MIMO) formulation that improves downstream accuracy without increasing decode latency. Benchmarks: outperforms transformer baselines by nearly 4% on language modeling while running up to 7x faster at long sequences (16K+ tokens); at 1.5B scale with MIMO, improves average downstream accuracy by 1.8 points over the next-best alternative. State size is half of Mamba-2 at comparable perplexity. Licensed Apache 2.0; code, weights, and custom CUDA/Triton kernels published on GitHub. Gaining significant Hacker News traction today (221 points, 43 comments).

**Today's Signal:** Mamba-3 is the clearest demonstration yet that the "inference-first" design philosophy — optimizing for the deployed, post-training world rather than the pre-training benchmark world — can produce a credible alternative to transformers at practical sequence lengths. For builders running long-context workloads at scale, the 7x inference speedup is not a rounding error.

## What Builders Are Talking About

**OpenCode as a case study in provider-agnostic agent design.** The HN thread isn't just celebrating the star count — it's a detailed discussion of OpenCode's architecture: multi-session support, ACP compatibility across JetBrains, Zed, Neovim, and Emacs, and the decision to never store code or context. The recurring point: the most-discussed pain point in the Claude Code ecosystem (vendor lock-in and API cost unpredictability after Anthropic blocked subscription access via third-party tools in January) became OpenCode's fastest growth vector. Builders are treating it as a reference design for how to build agent tooling that survives platform policy changes.

**Mamba-3 and the inference bottleneck.** The HN thread on Mamba-3 is generating practical discussion about the "cold GPU" problem: during decoding, hardware often waits for memory movement rather than computing. Mamba-3's MIMO formulation addresses this at the architectural level rather than through engineering hacks. Builders working on long-context workloads are noting the 7x inference speedup makes previously impractical sequence lengths economically viable in production.

**News publishers blocking Internet Archive.** An EFF piece arguing that blocking the Internet Archive to prevent AI scraping will erase the web's historical record is generating 302 points and 86 comments on HN. The core argument: the New York Times, The Guardian, and the Financial Times have blocked Internet Archive crawlers based on theoretical AI scraping risks — without documented evidence of scraping through the Wayback Machine — at the cost of permanent historical preservation. Commenters are debating whether robots.txt-based access control is a viable long-term content monetization strategy or a one-way door toward a less archivable web.

**Today's Signal:** All three threads converge on the same structural problem: institutions and platforms making unilateral decisions about access, control, and architecture that downstream users and builders then have to route around. OpenCode is the most successful routing-around story of the week. The unresolved pain point is that none of these workarounds — multi-provider agents, inference-optimized SSMs, archive mirrors — scale as clean replacements for the original access they're substituting.

## Discussions & Debates

**The Pentagon-Anthropic contradiction.** The Hacker News and tech press discussion of the new sworn declarations is less about AI policy substance and more about process: if a senior DoD official emailed the CEO of Anthropic saying the sides were "very close" on the day after finalizing a supply-chain risk designation — and then publicly declared no negotiation was happening — the debate isn't just about whether Anthropic poses a national security risk. It's about whether the designation was a negotiating tactic or a policy decision, and what that means for any AI company trying to navigate federal procurement. The March 24 hearing before Judge Rita Lin in San Francisco is the next pressure point.

**The Internet Archive as a proxy for the open web.** The EFF argument — that blocking the Internet Archive doesn't stop AI companies from scraping content through other means, but does permanently damage historical preservation — is generating a debate that extends well beyond the specific publishers involved. The core tension: publishers are responding to a real incentive problem (AI training on their content without compensation) with a blunt instrument (blocking a nonprofit preservationist) whose costs fall on future historians and researchers rather than on AI labs. Several commenters note that the Wayback Machine's presence in the Google C4 training dataset was already a fait accompli; blocking it now doesn't unwind past training.

**Today's Signal:** Both debates are about the same structural condition: decisions made under competitive pressure, with costs that accrue elsewhere. The Pentagon designated Anthropic without concluding negotiations; publishers blocked the Internet Archive without evidence of active scraping. The AI industry is creating enough urgency that institutions are choosing action over process — and the accountability for those choices is landing on third parties.
