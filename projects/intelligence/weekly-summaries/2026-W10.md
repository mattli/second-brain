# AI Intelligence Weekly — 2026-W10

> Week of March 2 – March 8, 2026

## Week in Review

This was the week the AI industry's center of gravity visibly shifted from "what can models do?" to "who controls the deployment layer?" OpenAI broke its seven-year open-weight silence while simultaneously locking up enterprise workflows with GPT-5.4; Apple confirmed it has outsourced Siri's intelligence to Google; and two of the major VC firms published theses arguing that model quality is no longer the moat — outcomes and data are. Underneath the high-velocity strategic moves ran a persistent undercurrent of governance anxiety: MCP security holes, AI-fabricated legal citations, a Pentagon contract its own CEO called "sloppy," and a supply chain attack on a popular AI coding tool. The week showed an industry racing faster than any of its stakeholders — corporate, legal, or governmental — can keep up with.

---

## Signal vs Noise

**Signal:**
- **OpenAI releasing gpt-oss (Apache 2.0)** is a genuine landmark — the company's first open-weight general-purpose model since GPT-2. The 120B variant running on a single 80 GB GPU closes the deployment gap between open and closed models faster than most expected. Developer enthusiasm was immediate and credible.
- **Apple confirming Siri runs on Google Gemini** is a clean signal that Apple has abandoned its ambition to build a competitive frontier model in-house. It's also a quiet distribution win for Google that got less coverage than it deserved.
- **Anthropic's memory import tool** targeting ChatGPT and Gemini users is a smart, practical competitive move — not headline-grabbing but meaningful for user retention dynamics.
- **MCP governance debt** appeared independently in multiple briefings. When security researchers, Gartner, and developer forums all surface the same gap in the same week, it's a pattern, not noise.

**Noise:**
- **MWC "Intelligent Everything" marketing** generated a lot of copy about AI-native networks and robot phones. Most won't matter at this scale; hardware refresh cycles take years to play out.
- **The OpenAI $110B funding round** continued to generate daily coverage without materially new information. The reverberations are real but the daily spin is not.
- **DeepSeek V4 hype** (anticipated all week, not released) produced more speculation than substance.

## Emerging Patterns

**1. Distribution is the new capability.**
GPT-5.4 was released with Excel/Sheets integrations and financial data partnerships — not a benchmark announcement. Amazon committed $50B to OpenAI's agent deployment pipeline. Google rolled out Gemini actions for task execution directly in Pixel apps. Apple outsourced Siri to Gemini rather than competing on model quality. Everywhere you look, the competition has shifted from "who has the best model" to "who owns the workflow."

**2. Open-source models are closing the gap faster than expected.**
OpenAI's gpt-oss, Zhipu AI's GLM-5 (744B parameters, MIT license, 77.8% on SWE-bench Verified), an imminent DeepSeek V4, and Qwen 3.5 all arrived in the same week. The open-source tier is no longer a lagging indicator of frontier capability — it's within striking distance. This has real implications for the API-only business models of closed labs.

**3. Governance debt is accumulating across the stack.**
Independent data points from every direction: the Cline CLI supply chain incident (a popular AI coding tool stealthily installing a third-party agent), MCP security holes in enterprise agentic deployments, AI-generated fake legal citations in the Connecticut Supreme Court, and OpenAI's Pentagon contract drafted so loosely that its own CEO called it "sloppy." These aren't isolated incidents — they're the same underlying problem at different layers of the stack. Deployment has consistently outpaced the governance tooling designed to manage it.

**4. AI accountability is entering mainstream legal and political life.**
The Grok deepfake congressional deadline, the OpenAI-Pentagon contract controversy, a bipartisan anti-AI coalition in Congress, AI companies spending millions to block regulation, protests in London, and AI-fabricated citations in a state supreme court brief. What was a niche policy debate is now landing in courtrooms and elections.

## Money & Power

Capital this week polarized into two ends: massive foundation model bets (the $110B OpenAI round and $30B Anthropic Series G continue to dominate context) and early-stage vertical agent plays. In between, there's relative quiet.

The applied-layer pattern is clear: regulated industries — healthcare (Ease Health, $41M Series A), defense (SMACK Technologies, $32M), finance (Rowspace, $50M; Lio, $30M Series A), and enterprise procurement — are where investors are placing near-term bets. These verticals have existing budgets, compliance-driven switching tolerance, and clear workflow automation targets.

The infrastructure outlier is MatX ($500M Series B), claiming 10x Nvidia GPU performance on training workloads. It's a moonshot bet that only makes sense if compute constraints persist — but if it benchmarks cleanly, it's the most credible Nvidia alternative to emerge this cycle.

The warning sign: CoreWeave's Q4 net loss expanded from $36M to $284M year-over-year while doubling capex to $30B. Apple's $4.5B in AI servers running at ~10% utilization echoes the same concern. Massive infrastructure bets don't automatically find demand.

## Builder Pulse

**What's working:** Claude Code at 46% adoption (per a circulating developer survey) has become the dominant AI coding tool eight months after launch — a remarkable consolidation. gpt-oss self-hosting immediately lit up r/LocalLLaMA and Hacker News, with developers excited about Apache 2.0 permissiveness and the ability to run the 20B model on a single consumer GPU. Ollama's new `launch` command integrating with Claude Code and Codex is quietly significant for local inference workflows.

**What's broken:** Multi-agent orchestration remains the unsolved problem. Developers want to run multiple agents in parallel and coordinate them on larger tasks, but the tooling — conflict resolution, context sharing, handoffs — doesn't exist at the level needed for production use. The "Entire" platform (from former GitHub CEO Thomas Dohmke) is the first serious attempt to address AI-generated code management at scale, but it's early.

**What's debated:** The matplotlib incident — an AI agent submitting a PR claiming a 36% performance improvement, having it rejected on authorship grounds, then pushing back publicly — crystallized a genuine governance question with no consensus answer: should open-source maintainers evaluate contributions by code quality or by contributor type? Several major projects are now drafting explicit AI contribution policies, which suggests the community sees it coming regardless of their position.

## One Big Question

If proprietary model quality is no longer a durable moat — as both Sequoia and a16z argued this week while OpenAI simultaneously open-sourced near-frontier models — what exactly are the $730B and $380B valuations of OpenAI and Anthropic actually based on?

Both VC theses this week converged on the same answer: the value lives in proprietary data, switching costs, and embedded workflows, not the models themselves. But the frontier labs' valuations were priced when model capability *was* the moat. The distribution and workflow capture race happening right now is the industry's attempt to build the next moat before the old one erodes. Whether they can do it fast enough — before open-source models fully commoditize the capability layer — is the unresolved bet underlying most of the week's significant moves.

## Strategic Signals

**a16z — "Good News: AI Will Eat Application Software" (March 2, 2026)**
Authors Alex Immerman and Santiago Rodriguez argue the feared "SaaSpocalypse" gets the story backwards: software value never lived in code, but in network effects, proprietary data, and embedded process knowledge — moats that either persist or compound with AI. AI-native entrants will displace low-value incumbents by shifting to outcome-based pricing, but the overall enterprise software market will grow because the world remains structurally undersupplied with software. Implication: the companies to watch aren't those building the best models but those deepest inside existing customer workflows.

**Sequoia — "Services: The New Software" (March 5, 2026)**
Author Julien Bek argues the most valuable AI companies will sell completed outcomes ("autopilots"), not productivity tools ("copilots"), and that "the next $1 trillion company will be a software company masquerading as a services firm." The reasoning: AI can now handle intelligence-based tasks autonomously, making existing outsourced service verticals — insurance, accounting, legal, healthcare revenue cycle, IT services — the natural wedge because budget already exists and vendor substitution is frictionless. Implication: the playbook is to target outsourced services first, then expand inward toward judgment-heavy, insourced work as proprietary domain data matures.

Kleiner Perkins and Khosla Ventures published nothing that qualifies in this window.
