One of the most exciting things about working on NanoClaw is that by customizing an open-source personal AI assistant, I'm learning how I actually work. No two people operate the same way, so the system molds around your use cases. It helps you understand how you think, what you prioritize. It's reflective.

The fact that it's endlessly customizable is both the gift and the trap. Every time I want to use it, I have to think "How am I going to use it?" That friction is stressful, but it's also the point. I'm not configuring software. I'm making decisions about how I want to manage my life, and those decisions reveal things about me that I wouldn't have discovered otherwise.

The tension is real, though. For the system to really serve me, I have to test it, iterate on it, and allow it to evolve. That's basically finding product-market fit where I'm the market. The product is molding around my own use cases and it's about feeling my way through it.
