Making a to-do list for the next day forces a confrontation with yourself. When I started doing it every evening, the hardest part wasn't the tasks — it was the mundane decisions. What am I eating for breakfast? Do I need to go to the store? Should therapy go on the list even though I'll never forget it?

My therapist said planning meals might help me not have to think about these things in the moment. She was right, but the planning itself is its own kind of work. "Okay, okay, silence. I don't know what to do. This process is challenging me to do what this process is challenging me to do: actually plan ahead."

I removed the AM/PM split because it was just another thing to manage. I stopped putting guitar on the list because I play throughout the day anyway. I learned that recurring items (read digest, read Infinite Jest) create useful redundancy — they're not tasks to complete but rhythms to maintain.

The weekly summary was the payoff. Seeing what I actually did versus what I planned — the gap between intention and action — is the most honest feedback loop I have. It's more useful than any productivity system because it's mine, in my voice, tracking what I actually care about.
