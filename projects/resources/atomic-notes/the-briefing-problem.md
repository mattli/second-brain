I built a daily AI briefing system and then spent weeks doubting whether it's actually useful. The core tension: I can customize the sources and the format, I can store the data and synthesize it, I can ask follow-up questions. But I keep wondering if I'd be better off just reading someone else's newsletter.

The advantages of my own briefing are real in theory: I specify the sources, I can iterate on the format, the data feeds into my weekly and monthly summaries, it compounds. But the sources are the persistent problem. The briefing makes assumptions I don't catch (calling a16z a "new entrant"), includes technical details I don't understand (KV cache compression), and has redundancy between sections.

What I actually want from it: stay informed and identify product trends. The product section was the weakest part. I iterated on it for weeks — adding "What Builders Are Talking About," trying new tools sections, splitting consumer vs. developer tools. It got better but I never felt like it was giving me what newsletters written by humans with real judgment provide.

The meta-lesson: building your own intelligence pipeline forces you to articulate what you actually care about, which is valuable even when the output is imperfect. The process of tweaking the briefing taught me more about my information needs than any newsletter ever would.
