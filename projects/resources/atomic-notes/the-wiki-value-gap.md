The problem with a personal knowledge base isn't building it. It's that having one doesn't automatically produce value. "I have a wiki. I don't know what questions to ask it."

Gary Tan asks G-Brain questions because being a VC generates questions naturally. Most people don't have that forcing function. You save things, organize them, watch the pages grow, and then sit there staring at it wondering what it's for. The wiki becomes a graveyard not because the data is bad but because there's no relationship with it.

I realized mine is built for compounding AI context (the Karpathy pattern) but I was treating it like external memory or a synthesis surface. That mismatch is why it felt inert. Six honest categories of what wikis are good for: external memory (low value), synthesis surface (high value but rare), compounding context for AI, writing pipeline, decision trail, and the graveyard. The graveyard is the default outcome.

The fix might be structural. One idea that stuck: agent-per-folder, where each wiki section gets its own agent with standing instructions. "Which agent do I want to talk to" is a question my brain can answer. "What folder do I want to open" is not. That reframes the wiki from a thing you query into a place where characters live. Whether that's real or just aesthetically appealing is something I'm still sitting with.
