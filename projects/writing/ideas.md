### Why I Play Guitar in the Morning

Playing guitar in the morning keeps me present. It's a good way to start the day doing something that engages your ability to listen, to think, and to keep your eyes open — after all, you just spent eight hours with them closed.

Alan Watts said the best way to get into a meditative state is to listen. This maps to something I've noticed coming out of meditation or a sensory deprivation tank: what I've always described as "hearing the silence" is probably just the mental chatter subsiding. Listening actively — really attending to sound — seems to get you there.

Reading can get you into a focused state too, but it feels wrong to go immediately into another world when you've just come out of sleep. There's also research suggesting that even indirect sunlight in your eyes early in the day improves how you feel. Guitar hits all of this: it's active, embodied, quiet, and present.

### The Beatles as an Expression of Art
The Beatles are to music what the Turkish get-up is to strength training — a single form that contains everything. Just as the TGU expresses the full range of human movement in one movement, the Beatles blended rock, folk, classical, Indian, music hall, and psychedelia into a unified body of work that functions as a complete artistic expression.

Related idea: there's something to be said for deep immersion in a single art form rather than constantly chasing novelty. It's easy to get addicted to the new — new genres, new artists, new techniques — but real understanding comes from going deep. This connects to a Buddhist concept: the trap of novelty-seeking as a form of craving, and the richness that comes from staying with one thing long enough to truly know it.
