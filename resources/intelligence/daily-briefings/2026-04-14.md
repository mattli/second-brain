**GPT-6 (Spud) did not launch today.** The widely circulated April 14 date — reported by multiple AI tracking sources and noted in yesterday's briefing — came and went without an OpenAI blog post, tweet, or announcement of any kind. **Someone bought 30+ WordPress plugins and planted a backdoor in all of them.** Austin Ginder at Anchor Hosting documented a sophisticated, large-scale supply chain attack: a buyer identified as "Kris" — with a background in SEO, crypto, and online gambling marketing — acquired the "Essential Plugin" portfolio of 30+ free WordPress plugins on Flippa for six figures in early 2025. His very first SVN commit planted a PHP deserialization backdoor in August 2025. The backdoor sat dormant for eight months before being activated April 5–6, 2026. The attack's command-and-control server resolved its domain through an Ethereum smart contract — making traditional domain takedowns useless. WordPress.org permanently closed all 31 plugins on April 7 and forced auto-updates on April 8, but the forced update removed the phone-home mechanism only, not the injected code in `wp-config.php`. The story is trending on Hacker News today with 1027 points and 293 comments. It is the second documented case in two weeks of a trusted plugin being acquired on Flippa and weaponized — same playbook, larger scale.

**Inertia Enterprises signs three agreements with Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory to commercialize laser-based fusion.** TechCrunch reported today that the startup — founded by NIF (National Ignition Facility) scientist Annie Kritcher, who helped design the 2022 experiment that first achieved fusion energy breakeven — has signed two strategic partnerships and one cooperative research and development agreement with LLNL, licensing approximately 200 patents. The agreements cover advanced laser development and fuel target improvements. Inertia raised a $450M Series A in February, making it one of the best-capitalized fusion startups. The NIF's laser-based inertial confinement approach is the only fusion method that has demonstrated scientific breakeven; the question is whether new, more efficient lasers can make it economically viable at grid scale.

**Today's Signal:** The day's biggest story is an absence: GPT-6 not launching on the date the AI tracking community had converged on represents a small but notable erosion of the signal reliability of insider leak networks. The WordPress attack — second in two weeks, same acquisition playbook, escalating scale — is the clearer signal: the open-source plugin marketplace's lack of any ownership-transfer review mechanism is now an actively exploited attack surface, and AI tools (the writeup explicitly credits Claude Code for the patch methodology) are being used on both sides of the security line. Inertia's LLNL agreements are the quieter but more durable story: if the energy constraint on AI compute is real, fusion commercialization is a 10-year bet that matters.

## Funding & Product News

**GitHub launches native Stacked PRs.** GitHub shipped Stacked PRs today, available at github.github.com/gh-stack/ via the `gh` CLI. The feature — 767 HN points and 417 comments — allows developers to split large changes into smaller, sequentially dependent pull requests that can be reviewed and merged independently. Stacked diffs have been the preferred workflow at companies like Meta (which uses Phabricator-derived tooling) for years; startups like Graphite have built businesses around the gap. GitHub's native implementation is the first direct acknowledgment that the existing PR model — one flat branch, one review — breaks under the kind of large, incremental change that AI-assisted coding tools routinely produce.

**DaVinci Resolve 21 announced at NAB 2026 (Las Vegas, April 13)** — the major update adds a Photo page, bringing Hollywood color grading tools (node-based grades, qualifiers, Power Windows, curves, professional scopes) to still photography. AI features include IntelliSearch for image libraries, AI SuperScale upscaling, and Resolve FX AI tools for subject isolation and skin refinement. The move is a direct attack on Adobe Lightroom and Capture One's core markets. DaVinci Resolve is free for the base version.

**Today's Signal:** Both product stories are incumbents or platform holders absorbing adjacent markets that upstarts had begun to own — GitHub absorbing the stacked-diff workflow that Graphite was building a business around, Blackmagic absorbing the high-end photo editing market that Capture One and Lightroom dominate. The pattern is consistent with the broader enterprise AI embedding play: platforms adding capabilities that were previously reasons to use a specialist tool, making the specialist tool's independent existence harder to justify. GitHub Stacked PRs is the more structurally significant of the two given its direct relevance to agentic coding workflows.

## Discussions & Debates

**"Ask HN: I quit my job over weaponized robots to start my own venture"** — a developer reports quitting a robotics company two weeks ago after discovering plans to mount teleoperated weapons on Boston Dynamics and Unitree robotic platforms for a customer demo. The poster writes that they felt unable to continue once they understood what the hardware would be used for, and is now starting a robotics venture they can "build with a clear conscience." The thread (68 pts, 38 comments) is early but the question it surfaces is one the AI and robotics community has circled for years: what individual engineers do when employers ask them to cross ethical lines they were not hired to cross. The 2022 Boston Dynamics open letter pledging not to weaponize robots — signed by Unitree among others — makes the story sharper: one of the signatories was later found to be integrating with Chinese military procurement infrastructure, and now individual engineers at similar companies are making individual exit decisions.

**"Multi-Agentic Software Development Is a Distributed Systems Problem (AGI can't save you from it)"** (kirancodes.me, 56 pts, 21 comments) — a formal systems researcher argues that the common "just wait for smarter models" response to multi-agent AI coordination failures is technically wrong. The essay formally models multi-agent software synthesis as a distributed consensus problem and sketches how the FLP (Fischer-Lynch-Paterson) impossibility theorem and Lamport's Byzantine Generals result apply directly to LLM agent systems: no matter how intelligent the agents, you cannot simultaneously guarantee safety, liveness, and fault tolerance; and if more than (n-1)/3 agents misunderstand the prompt, consensus is impossible. The core claim: these bounds are invariant to model capability. Smarter models shrink the constants; they don't remove the constraints. The essay is a pointed rebuttal to the "AGI will solve coordination" assumption embedded in several popular multi-agent frameworks.

**Today's Signal:** Both threads probe the same underlying question: what happens when you skip the formal groundwork. The robotics engineer is dealing with the human version — no mechanism to review what a company's hardware will actually be used for before an engineer is already building it. The distributed systems essay is dealing with the software version — multi-agent AI systems being shipped without formal protocols for the coordination failures that are mathematically guaranteed to occur. In both cases, the answer being implied is the same: the norms and frameworks need to be built in advance, not after the harm arrives.
