# AI Intelligence Briefing — 2026-04-29

## Top News

**Elon Musk took the stand at the OpenAI trial on April 28 and will return for a second day of testimony today.** Testifying at the federal courthouse in Oakland, Musk told the jury that "it is not OK to steal a charity" and warned that if OpenAI and Altman prevail, it would "give license to looting every charity in America." He described OpenAI's founding as a direct response to Larry Page calling him a "specieist" for prioritizing human survival over AI, and testified that he quit OpenAI in 2017 because other founders demanded too much equity as the nonprofit contemplated a for-profit arm — which he said he wanted to keep small. CNBC is running live updates as Musk returns to the stand for day 3 today. Reuters and the New York Times covered day 2 in detail.

**OpenAI and Amazon Web Services announced Bedrock Managed Agents, a managed AI agent service exclusive to AWS, powered by OpenAI frontier models.** In a Stratechery interview published April 29, OpenAI CEO Sam Altman and AWS CEO Matt Garman described the product as OpenAI's frontier models packaged inside AWS's native agent runtime — with AWS identity, permissions, logging, governance, and deployment — running on Trainium chips, with customer data staying within the customer's VPC. Altman confirmed the managed service is exclusive to AWS as a joint product, distinct from simply calling OpenAI's API from AWS. The announcement is the first direct product consequence of Monday's Microsoft-OpenAI partnership restructure that ended exclusivity and allowed OpenAI to serve on other cloud providers. The Stratechery interview also includes AWS CEO Garman describing how this co-built product differs from Amazon's existing AgentCore infrastructure and why AWS's historically neutral platform strategy positioned it well for the agentic moment.

**Google signed a classified AI deal with the Pentagon, becoming the third major AI company to give the Department of Defense access after Anthropic's refusal.** TechCrunch reported April 28 that Google expanded DoD access to its AI for classified networks, "essentially allowing all lawful uses," following OpenAI and xAI who signed similar deals in March. Google's contract includes language expressing it does not intend its AI to be used for domestic mass surveillance or autonomous weapons — similar contract language to OpenAI's — but the Wall Street Journal notes it is unclear whether such provisions are legally binding or enforceable. More than 950 Google employees have signed an open letter (notdivided.org) asking Google to follow Anthropic's lead. Google did not respond to TechCrunch's request for comment.

**Wiz Research disclosed a critical remote code execution (RCE) vulnerability in GitHub's internal git infrastructure.** CVE-2026-3854, published April 28, showed that any authenticated GitHub user could execute arbitrary commands on GitHub's backend servers using a single standard `git push` command, by exploiting an injection flaw in how push options were handled via the `X-Stat` header's last-write-wins semantics. GitHub.com was patched within 6 hours of Wiz's private disclosure; patches for GitHub Enterprise Server are available, but Wiz reports 88% of Enterprise instances remain unpatched at time of publication. The disclosure arrived on the same day Mitchell Hashimoto — GitHub user #1299, creator of Vagrant and HashiCorp co-founder — published a post announcing that Ghostty, his terminal emulator project, will leave GitHub after years of concerns about platform reliability and trust. The Register covered the latter under the headline "HashiCorp co-founder says GitHub 'no longer a place for serious work.'" Together, the two events drove the day's largest Hacker News thread: 2795 points and 828 comments.

**Today's Signal:** The Microsoft-OpenAI restructure announced Monday is already materializing as a concrete product: Bedrock Managed Agents gives AWS enterprise customers a fully integrated OpenAI agent stack for the first time, with governance baked in rather than bolted on. The Google Pentagon deal, meanwhile, closes the gap on who has said yes to the DoD — it's now everyone except Anthropic. The convergence of a critical GitHub RCE and a prominent open source maintainer's departure from the platform on the same day raises a real question about concentration risk in developer infrastructure, at precisely the moment when AI agents are being given live access to those same infrastructure systems.

## Funding & Product News

**Scout AI raised $100 million in a Series A, described as the largest defense-tech Series A in U.S. history.** Announced April 29, the round was co-led by Align Ventures and Draper Associates, with participation from Decisive Point, Booz Allen Ventures, BVVC, and others. Scout AI builds Fury, a foundation model for unmanned warfare that is designed to serve as the decision-making layer for fleets of autonomous drones and ground vehicles across air, land, sea, and space — allowing a single operator to command many robots. The company has booked $11 million in contracts with the U.S. Department of War and has publicly demonstrated a fully autonomous AI-executed strike mission. Founded 18 months ago by Colby Adcock (brother of Figure AI CEO Brett Adcock) and Collin Otis, Scout has a 34-person team in Sunnyvale, California. TechCrunch covered the raise with a site visit to Scout's training facility.

**Today's Signal:** Scout AI's raise and the Google Pentagon deal on the same day make visible something that has been moving more quietly than the consumer AI wave: frontier AI is entering the autonomous weapons supply chain at the foundational model layer, not just as a bolt-on targeting assistant. The question of whether U.S. AI labs should supply the DoD with unrestricted access to their models — a question Anthropic answered differently than everyone else — is now also a question about whether those models will directly coordinate weapons systems. Those are meaningfully different things.

## Discussions & Debates

**"Who owns the code Claude Code wrote?" generated 439 points and 403 comments on Hacker News, driven by a legal analysis from Legal Layer that connects to a real incident.** The piece, by attorney Sena Evren (published April 28), was triggered by Anthropic accidentally publishing 512,000 lines of Claude Code's source code in a routine software update in early 2026 — a codebase that Anthropic's own lead engineer publicly acknowledged was predominantly written by Claude itself. Before the DMCA takedowns began, the codebase was mirrored on GitHub and a developer rewrote the entire thing in Python; the "claw-code" repository hit 100,000 stars in a single day. The legal analysis walks through three distinct IP problems for developers shipping AI-assisted code: code generated without meaningful human authorship may not be copyrightable by anyone under current doctrine (the Copyright Office's position, upheld in Thaler and not reviewed by the Supreme Court, which denied cert in March 2026); employment contracts with broad IP assignment clauses may give employers claims over personal side projects built with company-licensed tools like Claude Code or Cursor; and AI tools may reproduce verbatim portions of GPL-licensed training data, creating copyleft obligations in commercial codebases that are impossible to detect without a dedicated license scan.

**"How ChatGPT serves ads — here's the full attribution loop" generated 389 points and 256 comments.** A researcher at buchodi.com published a technical reverse engineering of ChatGPT's advertising infrastructure, captured from a consented mobile-traffic research panel. Key findings: ads are injected as typed `single_advertiser_ad_unit` objects directly into the conversation's SSE (Server-Sent Events) stream while the model is responding; targeting appears fully contextual (same account, six different conversation topics, six different advertisers — Grubhub for a Beijing trip, GetYourGuide for Beijing tour bookings, Gametime for NBA playoffs); four Fernet-encrypted click tokens per ad handle spam integrity and attribution; OpenAI hosts all advertiser creative on its own CDN (`bzrcdn.openai.com`); and links open in ChatGPT's in-app webview, meaning OpenAI observes post-click navigation on top of any pixel signal from the merchant. The HN thread is less about the existence of ads — which OpenAI has disclosed — and more about what the implementation reveals: advertising infrastructure is baked directly into the model's response stream schema, not layered above it, which has implications for ad blocking, client-side modification, and the relationship between the model's outputs and its commercial interests.

**Today's Signal:** Both threads are asking versions of the same question: when AI does something previously done by humans — writing code, recommending products — what legal and commercial machinery travels with it that was never there before? The code ownership question is about IP law catching up to agentic workflows. The ads question is about whether users can reasonably distinguish between model outputs and commercial placements when they are structurally identical objects in the same response stream.
