# AI Intelligence Briefing — 2026-05-13

## Top News

**Anthropic is in talks to raise $30–50 billion at a valuation of up to $950 billion, which would for the first time place it above OpenAI's $852 billion valuation.** Bloomberg reported the discussions on May 12; the New York Times confirmed with sources and added that the round could close as soon as the end of May, though no term sheet has been signed. The proposed valuation is 2.5× Anthropic's February 2026 Series G valuation of $380 billion — the same round that was itself a doubling — and follows Dario Amodei's disclosure at Anthropic's developer conference last week that the company reached a $30 billion annualized revenue run rate after growing 80× in Q1. The scale of the raise is driven not only by revenue momentum but by forward capital commitments: Anthropic has locked in $200 billion in Google Cloud spend and structured a $1.5 billion JV with Blackstone, Goldman Sachs, Hellman & Friedman, and General Atlantic, and its compute agreements with Google and Broadcom reportedly run into the hundreds of billions.

**A Chinese think tank representative approached Anthropic officials at a Carnegie Endowment for International Peace conference in Singapore last month and asked for access to Mythos — Anthropic refused, and the approach alarmed officials at the U.S. National Security Council.** The New York Times reported the incident on May 12, noting that the outreach was not an official government demand but was interpreted by senior administration officials as a signal of how Beijing intends to pursue access to US frontier models. Separately, Japan's Kyodo News reported today that Japan's government is in discussions with Anthropic to obtain access to Mythos for cyberattack defense purposes — a direct contrast with the Chinese approach. The China incident is the most concrete illustration to date of the access-control pressure Anthropic faces: Mythos is restricted to approximately 50 organizations globally and has already triggered responses from central banks, intelligence agencies, and the Pentagon, which declared the company a security risk earlier this year. Trump is scheduled to meet Xi in Beijing today, with AI policy among the structural issues in the relationship.

**TeamPCP, the threat actor responsible for previous Shai-Hulud supply chain campaigns, executed the largest coordinated npm/PyPI registry poisoning of 2026 on May 11 — compromising 170+ packages across TanStack (42 packages, 84 malicious versions), Mistral AI's npm and PyPI SDKs, 65 UiPath packages, the OpenSearch JavaScript client, and Guardrails AI.** The TanStack attack is the most technically novel element: the attacker did not steal npm credentials. Instead, they chained a `pull_request_target` "Pwn Request" misconfiguration, GitHub Actions cache poisoning across the fork/base trust boundary, and runtime extraction of an OIDC token directly from the Actions runner process memory — allowing malicious packages to be published under TanStack's legitimate trusted CI/CD identity. The malicious versions exfiltrate GitHub Actions secrets, npm publish tokens, cloud credentials, and SSH keys; a persistent daemon component requires disabling the monitor service before revoking credentials to avoid triggering home directory wipeout. TanStack published a full postmortem today; affected PyPI projects (including Mistral AI and Guardrails AI) remain quarantined. This is the fifth Shai-Hulud wave in eight months.

**Walmart is cutting or relocating approximately 1,000 corporate workers, consolidating its global technology and AI product teams under a single organizational structure.** Bloomberg and Reuters reported the move on May 12, citing an internal memo from head of global technology Suresh Kumar and head of global AI acceleration Daniel Danker. Unlike the Cloudflare, Coinbase, and Freshworks restructurings of the past week — which cited AI-made roles redundant — Walmart's stated rationale is operational simplification: teams working on overlapping problems, with the reduction targeting roles that will be absorbed into unified tech/AI reporting lines. Affected workers can apply for other internal openings; many have been asked to relocate to Bentonville or Northern California. Walmart's global headcount (~2.1 million, mostly hourly) is expected to remain flat over three years per CEO John Furner's guidance.

**Today's Signal:** The Anthropic $950 billion valuation talks and the Singapore access-control incident are the same story at two different layers: the company is worth nearly a trillion dollars precisely because its frontier model is restricted enough that foreign governments are sending think-tank representatives to request access through backdoor channels. The Mini Shai-Hulud attack is a significant CI/CD security inflection — the attack didn't require stolen credentials, only a misconfigured GitHub Actions trust boundary. That technique is now documented, detailed, and available to any threat actor reading the TanStack postmortem.

## Funding & Product News

**Cerebras Systems is expected to price its Nasdaq IPO this evening (May 13) at $150–$160 per share — an increase from the original $115–$125 range — raising approximately $4.8 billion at a fully diluted valuation approaching $49 billion, with trading beginning tomorrow (May 14) under ticker CBRS.** The offering was oversubscribed more than 20 times, triggering two consecutive price-range increases. Cerebras reported $510 million in 2025 revenue (+76% year-over-year) and a 47% net margin — rare for a company at this growth stage. Its wafer-scale chip architecture, 58 times larger than Nvidia's B200 GPU, is purpose-built for inference: the company claims up to 15× faster inference than leading GPU alternatives. Key customers include OpenAI (a $20 billion compute deal) and Amazon Web Services. If pricing holds, Cerebras will be the largest IPO globally in 2026 to date and the most significant pure-play AI hardware listing since the current AI investment cycle began.

**Today's Signal:** The Cerebras IPO and the Anthropic $950 billion round are simultaneous capital events that together show how AI infrastructure demand is being priced by both private and public markets. At 20× oversubscription, Cerebras reflects the same logic that drove Anthropic's valuation 2.5× in three months: the market has concluded that the inference-hardware bottleneck is structural, not cyclical, and is pricing accordingly.

## Discussions & Debates

**James Shore's essay "You Need AI That Reduces Maintenance Costs" (jamesshore.com, May 10) has been generating sustained HN discussion over the past 48 hours.** Shore's argument is mathematical and blunt: if an AI coding agent doubles your code output, it must halve your maintenance cost per line, or you are worse off in the long run than if you had never used it. His model shows that doubling output while holding maintenance costs constant doubles total maintenance burden; doubling output while also doubling per-unit maintenance cost quadruples it. He argues that "current evidence suggests coding agents increase maintenance costs" — meaning most teams are currently making the trade without realizing the math works against them over a 2–3 year horizon. The HN discussion concentrated on whether the framing is correct (many commenters agreed with the maintenance-cost concern but disputed the linearity of the model), and on what "maintenance cost" even means for AI-generated code (readability, test coverage, reviewability, debugging surface area).

**The maintainers of RPCS3, the open-source PS3 emulator, posted a message asking contributors to stop submitting AI-generated pull requests without disclosing them, after the project was flooded with undisclosed AI-generated code.** Covered by Kotaku and reaching the HN front page today, the maintainers described reviewing a high volume of PRs that appeared to be AI-generated: syntactically plausible, often passing basic tests, but wrong in ways that require deep emulator knowledge to catch. The request asks contributors to disclose AI assistance, not to stop using it — but the volume and the hidden nature of the submissions is the reported problem. The thread connects directly to Shore's maintenance-cost argument: RPCS3 maintainers are experiencing the downstream cost of AI-generated code as a review burden and trust asymmetry, not as productivity gain.

**Today's Signal:** Shore's essay and the RPCS3 thread are asking the same question from different angles: who pays the cost of AI-generated code, and when? Shore frames it as a developer economics problem across the life of a codebase; RPCS3 frames it as a maintainer bandwidth problem on an open-source project. Both suggest that the productivity narrative around AI coding agents is currently measured at the point of code generation — which is the easiest moment to measure — and that the harder-to-measure costs show up later, distributed across the people who have to live with the code.
