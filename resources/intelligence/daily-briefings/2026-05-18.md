# AI Intelligence Briefing — 2026-05-18

## Top News

**The nine-person advisory jury in Musk v. Altman began deliberating today (May 18), with the remedies phase set to follow; Judge Gonzalez Rogers will issue the binding decision after receiving the advisory verdict.** NPR's Morning Edition reported the start of deliberations early this morning, picked up by NPR affiliates KGOU and KASU. The jury's central question is whether Musk filed his lawsuit within the statute of limitations — Judge Gonzalez Rogers stated in a court filing last month that if the jury finds Musk missed the filing window, she is "highly likely" to direct a verdict for the defendants. If the jury finds the suit was timely, it must then determine whether OpenAI's nonprofit-to-for-profit conversion violated charitable trust obligations and whether Altman and Brockman unjustly enriched themselves. Musk is seeking more than $134 billion in damages (to revert to OpenAI's nonprofit foundation, not to Musk personally), removal of Altman and Brockman, and invalidation of certain Microsoft deals. The advisory verdict could arrive as early as this week; the judge makes the final binding determination on both liability and remedies.

**Bloomberg's Mark Gurman published new details on Apple's Siri revamp in his Power On newsletter on May 17: the standalone Siri app will ship with auto-deleting conversation history (30 days, 1 year, or indefinitely), will run Google Gemini models on Apple's own private cloud compute infrastructure rather than Google's servers, and will launch at WWDC June 8 under a beta label.** Reported by 9to5Mac, MacRumors, and others on May 17. The privacy architecture is the structurally significant detail: Apple will run a custom Gemini-based model — known internally as Apple Foundation Models version 11, described as comparable to Gemini 3 — on its own servers under its Private Cloud Compute infrastructure, meaning Google will not receive user Siri data or use it for model training. The new app will have two interface modes: a ChatGPT-style conversation view, and a Messages-style conversation list. A system-wide gesture for entering new Siri chats is also planned. The beta label at public launch is consistent with Apple's approach to major feature rollouts under development — it signals the company is shipping before full polish rather than delaying until WWDC 2027. The Gemini-powered Siri ships in iOS 27 this fall.

**Google I/O opens tomorrow (May 19) at 10 a.m. PT at Shoreline Amphitheater in Mountain View, with a confirmed product slate and a new Gemini model expected as the keynote centerpiece — but pre-I/O reporting suggests the model will land near GPT-5.5's capability tier rather than challenging Claude Mythos.** TechTimes summarized the confirmed announcements on May 17: Googlebook (the merged Android/ChromeOS laptop platform, previously codenamed Aluminium OS), Android XR smart glasses (Google's first since Google Glass), and Gemini Intelligence (the agentic AI layer for Android). The Gemini model update — expected to be announced as Gemini 3.2, 3.5, or possibly 4.0 — is the product most watched by the AI community. Multiple outlets citing unnamed sources describe it as a meaningful improvement in reasoning and multimodal capability but not a "step change," particularly in coding benchmarks where Anthropic's Claude has become the developer default. A developer keynote follows at 1:30 p.m. PT. The I/O keynote is the most significant AI event on the calendar for the next 30 days; Thursday's briefing will cover what was actually released.

**Today's Signal:** The Musk v. Altman deliberations and tomorrow's Google I/O are the two most consequential events of the week — one settling a foundational question about AI lab governance and nonprofit conversion; the other setting Google's competitive positioning for the rest of 2026. The Apple Siri detail is the most substantive news of the day: the private-cloud compute architecture resolves the central tension in the Apple-Google Gemini deal by keeping user data inside Apple's infrastructure. That's a materially different product than "Apple ships Gemini" — it's Apple building a privacy moat around a third-party model.

## Discussions & Debates

**Jeff Geerling's May 12 essay "Bambu Lab is abusing the open source social contract" (jeffgeerling.com) has become the highest-engagement tech story on Hacker News today with 1,191 points and 380 comments — more than a week after publication, driven by the ongoing development of the underlying dispute.** Geerling, a prominent tech YouTuber and hardware blogger, argues that Bambu Lab — the dominant Chinese 3D printer manufacturer — is violating the AGPL-v3 open source license under which it ships Bambu Studio (its own slicer software, itself a fork of OrcaSlicer, which is a fork of PrusaSlicer). In April 2026, Bambu sent a cease-and-desist to Polish developer Paweł Jarczak, who had created an OrcaSlicer fork that restored direct-connection printer control that Bambu had disabled via its cloud-mandatory Authorization Control System. Bambu's legal response characterized the fork as "impersonation" via "falsified identity metadata" — because the fork identified itself as Bambu Studio when talking to Bambu's servers — rather than as an open-source rights dispute. Right-to-repair advocate Louis Rossmann publicly offered to pay Jarczak's legal fees. The HN discussion concentrated on the legal and ethical question Bambu's response sidesteps: AGPL requires that anyone who modifies and distributes the code must publish their changes and allow further modification — but Bambu is arguing its cloud back-end is "private infrastructure" not covered by AGPL, allowing it to accept AGPL's benefits (building on OrcaSlicer) while using terms-of-service enforcement to prevent the modifications AGPL explicitly authorizes. The delay between publication and HN peak engagement reflects the week of follow-on reporting that filled in the legal details Geerling's original post summarized.

**Today's Signal:** The Bambu Lab story is structurally the same issue as the hardware attestation debate from the May 12 briefing — a company using technical lock-in and legal pressure to control what users can do with hardware and software they purchased. Bambu's specific move is legally aggressive because it relies on AGPL to build its own software, then uses ToS enforcement against AGPL's central guarantee: the right to fork, modify, and use the modified version. If the "cloud infrastructure is private, AGPL covers only the local code" argument holds, it becomes a template for any company that wants the community development benefits of open source licensing while retaining platform control over how that code is actually used.
