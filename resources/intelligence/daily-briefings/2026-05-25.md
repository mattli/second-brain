# AI Intelligence Briefing — 2026-05-25

## Top News

**Pope Leo XIV published his first encyclical, Magnifica Humanitas ("Magnificent Humanity"), today — the first papal encyclical in history dedicated entirely to artificial intelligence — calling for strict regulation, warning that lethal autonomous weapons decisions are "not permissible," and presenting the document alongside Anthropic co-founder and interpretability researcher Christopher Olah.** Covered by the Washington Post, CNN, NBC News, PBS, National Catholic Reporter, and Vatican News on May 25. The document, which Leo signed on May 15 — exactly 135 years to the day after Leo XIII signed Rerum Novarum, the foundational Catholic teaching on workers' rights during the Industrial Revolution — argues that "merely regulating AI is insufficient" and calls for what it frames as "disarming AI": not rejecting technology but "preventing it from dominating humanity." Key positions: AI must not be "concentrated in the hands of only a few"; autonomous weapons systems make war "more 'feasible' and less subject to human control" and must face "the most rigorous ethical constraints"; calling for a slower pace of AI adoption "is not opposing progress — it is an exercise of responsible care for the human family." Leo directly ties the AI moment to the industrial revolution's transformation of labor: "The pursuit of greater profits cannot justify choices that systematically sacrifice jobs, because the human person is an end, not a means." Olah's presence at the Vatican press conference — Olah leads Anthropic's interpretability research team and co-invented the concept of neural network "circuits" — is the concrete institutional signal: this is not a generic moral statement but an engagement with the specific researchers building frontier AI. The encyclical reached 330 points and 111 comments on Hacker News today.

**Anthropic is described as "hours away" from closing a $30 billion funding round at a pre-money valuation above $900 billion — which, if completed this week, would make it the world's most valuable AI startup, surpassing OpenAI's $852 billion valuation — co-led by Sequoia Capital, Dragoneer Investment Group, Altimeter Capital, and Greenoaks Capital Partners, with participation from Founders Fund and General Catalyst.** Bloomberg reported on May 22 that the round would close "as soon as next week"; multiple outlets on May 25 characterize it as imminent. This represents a second $30 billion round in a single calendar year: Anthropic closed a $380 billion Series G in February 2026. The valuation trajectory is striking — $61 billion as recently as late 2025, $380 billion in February, and now $900 billion — tracking Anthropic's revenue curve: the company projected $10.9 billion in Q2 2026 revenue and its first-ever operating profit (covered May 21). Sequoia, Dragoneer, Altimeter, and Greenoaks are each expected to contribute approximately $2 billion; other existing investors are participating. A $900 billion valuation would place Anthropic alongside Apple, Nvidia, and Microsoft in the upper tier of global technology company valuations — as a private company still burning cash at the infrastructure layer.

**OpenAI filed its confidential S-1 registration statement with the SEC on May 22, formally opening the public-offering process and targeting a September 2026 IPO at a valuation between $852 billion and $1 trillion, with Goldman Sachs and Morgan Stanley leading the deal.** Reported by Enterprise DNA, Fortune, and multiple IPO-tracking outlets on May 22 — this filing was missed in the May 22 briefing, which covered the White House EO cancellation and other stories from the same day. The confidential filing means the financial details remain sealed until approximately 15 days before the public roadshow, giving the SEC time to review privately. The financial context is complex: OpenAI is reported to have lost $1.22 for every $1 of revenue in Q1 2026, meaning the IPO will price a company with dramatic revenue growth and proportional losses — a structure the market has accepted before (Amazon, Lyft, Palantir) but that will face intensive scrutiny at the roadshow. The September target is aggressive given the typical SEC review timeline; late 2026 or early 2027 is the more probable window if the review produces substantive comments.

**Today's Signal:** The simultaneous arrival of Pope Leo XIV's AI encyclical and the Anthropic/OpenAI financial milestones is not coincidental in a structural sense: the moral authority of the Catholic Church (1.4 billion people) is asserting, on the same day that the two most prominent AI labs are in the news for their valuations, that the accumulation of AI power in a few hands is an ethical problem requiring intervention. Leo's choice to present the encyclical alongside an Anthropic researcher rather than a government official or ethicist signals that the Vatican's strategy is direct engagement with the labs — not appeals to regulators who are themselves uncertain. Whether that engagement produces any concrete constraint on lab behavior is unknowable today; what is observable is that the moral vocabulary around AI concentration is becoming more specific and institutionally backed.

## Discussions & Debates

**George Hotz (geohot), the founder of Comma.ai and one of the most prominent practitioner-critics of mainstream AI development practices, published "The Eternal Sloptember" on May 24 — arguing that adopting AI coding agents for software development represents "one of the most costly mistakes in the field's history" — reaching 385 points and 312 comments as the top Hacker News story today.** Hotz's central claim: AI agents are "sophisticated statistical models that mimic the distribution of programming" rather than systems that understand code, and their output is "broken in a way that's getting harder and harder to detect." He identifies an organizational failure mode: high-performing engineers catch flawed agent output through careful review, but lower-performing workers produce "10x output" of defective code without verification — degrading team quality overall even as apparent productivity rises. He predicts AI agents will produce "more code, more apps, and more features than ever before" alongside a decline in what he calls "gem-like quality." The HN discussion split along familiar lines: practitioners citing dramatic personal productivity gains (one developer built a 72K-line project in two weeks); critics arguing that AI-assisted code defers architectural problems rather than resolving them, creating unmaintainable systems. The debate maps directly onto the Mitchell Hashimoto "AI psychosis" thread (covered May 20): both are practitioner-level critiques that acknowledge productivity gains while arguing the organizational and quality costs are being systematically undercounted.

**Epoch AI published analysis showing that high-bandwidth memory (HBM) has grown from 52% to 63% of total AI chip component costs between Q1 2024 and Q4 2025, with absolute HBM spending across Nvidia, AMD, Google, and Amazon growing from approximately $12 billion in 2024 to $32 billion in 2025 — making memory, not compute logic, the primary driver of AI infrastructure costs.** Published May 21, the analysis has reached approximately 410 points and 436 comments on Hacker News today in active discussion. The structural implication Epoch identifies: "Compute scarcity in 2026 is mostly a memory story." Logic dies (the processing chips themselves) remained relatively stable in cost share (~13%), while advanced packaging declined. HBM supply is concentrated at SK Hynix, Samsung, and Micron — a more constrained supplier base than the logic foundry market. The HN discussion focused on what this means for pricing power: if memory is now two-thirds of accelerator cost, memory supply constraints and pricing decisions by HBM manufacturers carry more leverage over AI infrastructure costs than TSMC's logic production decisions. This is a genuine hardware economics shift with pricing implications for every AI lab's compute budget.

**Today's Signal:** Hotz's "Eternal Sloptember" and Hashimoto's "AI psychosis" (May 20) are converging on the same diagnosis from different angles: the productivity gains from AI coding tools are real and measurable, but the quality and organizational costs are invisible in the short term and potentially catastrophic at scale. The Epoch AI memory analysis adds a hardware constraint layer: even if agent-generated code eventually meets quality standards, the compute infrastructure required to run it is becoming more concentrated in supply and more expensive in structure. The two debates together — can agents program well enough, and can the hardware scale cheaply enough — are the two critical open questions beneath the current AI-adoption wave.
