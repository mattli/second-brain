# AI Intelligence Briefing — 2026-03-25

## Top News

**Judge Lin delivers a verdict-in-waiting: Anthropic hearing goes sharply against the Pentagon.** In the 90-minute hearing covered in yesterday's briefing, U.S. District Judge Rita Lin went well beyond procedural skepticism — she called the DOD's actions "an attempt to cripple Anthropic" and said they "don't seem tailored to the national security concerns" the government cited. She pressed the Justice Department attorney directly: if the concern was the risk of Anthropic modifying its model, couldn't the DOD simply stop using Claude? The DOJ also conceded that Defense Secretary Hegseth's social media post ("this decision is final") was not legally binding — a position Lin called "pretty surprising" since it was seen by 13 million people. She requested additional filings by Wednesday and indicated a ruling before the end of the week. Lin's framing — "everyone agrees DOW can stop using Claude, the question is whether they violated the law by going further" — is the strongest public signal yet that Anthropic is likely to win the preliminary injunction.

**OpenAI shuts down Sora and dissolves its Disney deal.** In a surprising announcement Tuesday, OpenAI said it is shutting down its Sora AI video-creation app. The Wall Street Journal first reported the news; OpenAI confirmed it on X with "We're saying goodbye to Sora." The closure comes ahead of the company's expected IPO and reflects a deliberate resource reallocation: by shutting down the compute-heavy video generation service, OpenAI frees GPU capacity for more profitable coding, reasoning, and text tasks — exactly the categories where Anthropic has been taking enterprise market share. As part of the shutdown, the December deal with Disney — a $1 billion investment alongside a three-year content partnership — is being dissolved. Disney said it "respects OpenAI's decision to exit the video generation business." The Sora app had been the most-downloaded in the iOS Photo and Video category on launch day, but its resource requirements were never resolved: OpenAI had already begun rationing video generation in late 2025 due to chip constraints.

**Google Research publishes TurboQuant: 6x KV cache compression with zero accuracy loss.** Google Research released TurboQuant (March 24), a training-free compression algorithm for LLM key-value caches that achieves at least 6x memory reduction and up to 8x processing speedup on Nvidia H100 GPUs — with no accuracy degradation. The technique combines two algorithms: PolarQuant, which converts vector data into a polar coordinate form that eliminates storage overhead, and QJL (Quantized Johnson-Lindenstrauss), a 1-bit error-correction layer. No retraining or fine-tuning is required. The algorithm is already being implemented in MLX by the community. It will be presented at ICLR 2026 next month. For builders running large-context workloads, this addresses one of the most persistent cost scaling problems: KV cache memory growing proportionally with context window length.

**Anthropic launches Claude Code "auto mode" in research preview.** Anthropic released a new "auto mode" for Claude Code (TechCrunch, March 24) that shifts the decision of when to ask for user permission from the human to the AI itself. A safety layer reviews each action before execution, checking for risky behavior beyond the user's request and for prompt injection attacks — malicious instructions hidden in content being processed by the AI. Safe actions proceed automatically; risky ones are blocked. The feature is rolling out to Enterprise and API users and currently works only with Claude Sonnet 4.6 and Opus 4.6; Anthropic recommends sandboxed, isolated environments. The undisclosed criteria for what counts as "risky" is the open question developers will push on before adopting it widely.

**Today's Signal:** Today's pattern is a sequence of strategic recalibrations forced by competitive pressure: OpenAI exits video to survive the march toward IPO; Anthropic simultaneously gets an effective legal victory in the government dispute and ships a product that moves its core tool toward greater autonomy. The TurboQuant release is a quieter structural shift — if near-lossless 6x compression holds up in production, it changes the economics of long-context deployment across every major model, not just Google's. The day is less about new capabilities than about companies figuring out which bets are worth keeping.

## Funding & Launches

**Kleiner Perkins raises $3.5 billion, going "all in on AI."** The veteran venture firm closed a new $3.5 billion fund (TechCrunch, March 24), its largest in recent memory. The firm framed the raise explicitly as an AI-focused bet across the full stack: infrastructure, frontier models, and application layer. No specific portfolio disclosures yet.

**Today's Signal:** Kleiner Perkins raising at this scale is a signal that the largest traditional VC firms — not just new entrants like a16z — are repositioning AI as their primary thesis. Combined with last week's Gimlet Labs and Nebius raises, capital continues to flow toward infrastructure rather than applications. The pattern is consistent: money is hedging against model commoditization by owning the efficiency and deployment layer.

## Discussions & Debates

**HN "Goodbye to Sora" thread (808 points, 588 comments).** The discussion is less about video AI than about what the shutdown reveals about OpenAI's IPO calculus. The dominant thread: OpenAI is making a deliberate product portfolio bet — exit entertainment and consumer video, double down on coding and reasoning — precisely because that's where Anthropic is winning enterprise contracts. Several commenters noted the irony that Sora's 2024 launch, which "rattled Hollywood," ends not because of regulatory pressure or content controversy but because the compute wasn't worth it when stacked against tools that directly drive enterprise revenue. The dissolved Disney deal ($1B) is generating its own side thread about what high-profile partnership announcements actually mean in practice when the underlying product economics don't work.

**Cursor's Composer 2/Kimi K2.5 controversy (252 points, 138 comments on HN today).** The story broke March 19 when a developer found a model ID in Cursor's API — `kimi-k2p5-rl-0317-s515-fast` — revealing that Cursor's new Composer 2, promoted as "frontier-level coding intelligence" with benchmark charts against Opus 4.6 and GPT-5.4, was built on Kimi K2.5, an open-weight model from Beijing-based Moonshot AI. Cursor's VP of developer education acknowledged the base model but said only 25% of final compute came from Kimi; the rest was proprietary reinforcement learning. The thread today focuses on the valuation question: Cursor raised at $29.3B last fall partly on the promise of building its own models. Moonshot's Kimi K2.5 license requires companies with over $20M/month in revenue to display "Kimi K2.5" prominently in their product — Cursor, reportedly at $2B+ ARR (annualized recurring revenue), appears to meet that threshold. Legal is reportedly involved.

**Today's Signal:** Both debates map to the same unresolved question about AI product companies: what is the actual proprietary value when the models are open-weight, the base compute is commoditizing, and the differentiation is UI plus fine-tuning? Cursor's case puts the question in its sharpest form yet — if RL on an open-weight base can outperform frontier closed models on coding benchmarks, the moat is in distribution, data, and user behavior, not model training. That's a different story than what $50B valuations imply.
