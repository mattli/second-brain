# AI Intelligence Briefing — 2026-04-03

## Top News

**Perplexity sued for sharing users' complete AI chat transcripts with Meta and Google — even in "Incognito Mode."** A 135-page class-action complaint filed March 31 in federal court in San Francisco, reported in extensive technical detail by Ars Technica April 2, alleges Perplexity embeds Meta Pixel, Google Ads, and Google DoubleClick trackers that transmit complete user chat conversations — including sensitive financial, health, and legal content — to Meta and Google without user knowledge or consent. Non-subscribed users' entire prompts are shared via full-string URLs giving Meta and Google direct access to conversations. Perplexity's "Incognito Mode" is called a "sham" by the suit: even paid users who enabled the feature had their full conversations and email addresses shared. The complaint covers all users whose chats were allegedly shared between December 2022 and February 2026. The lawsuit arrives at a damaging moment: Perplexity is actively pitching "Computer" ($200/month agentic tool), "Personal Computer," and "Comet" browser — products that require users to grant even deeper personal data access. Perplexity says it hasn't been served the suit; Meta pointed to existing advertiser policies; Google said businesses are responsible for disclosing their own data practices.

**Two new Rowhammer attacks on Nvidia GPU GDDR memory give complete root control of host machines in shared cloud environments.** Ars Technica reported April 2 on "GDDRHammer" and "GeForge" — two novel attack techniques that demonstrate how a malicious user co-tenanting a shared cloud GPU instance can exploit DRAM (dynamic random-access memory) bit-flip vulnerabilities in GPU GDDR memory to escalate privileges and gain full root control of the host machine. Because high-performance Nvidia GPUs (typically $8,000+) are routinely shared among dozens of users in cloud environments, a single malicious tenant can take over the entire physical host, compromising the underlying CPU. No patches have been published.

**Alibaba releases three closed-source Qwen models in three days, completing a strategic pivot from open to proprietary.** Bloomberg reported April 2 that Alibaba released its third consecutive closed-source model: Qwen3.5-Omni (fully multimodal — text, image, audio, video — with a 256K token context window, voice cloning, and a closed license), Qwen3.6-Plus (agentic coding-focused), and a closed image-generation update. All three are exclusively available via Alibaba's cloud platform or API — none are downloadable. This marks a material strategic break: Alibaba spent two years building global developer mindshare by open-sourcing its Qwen models; it is now monetizing through cloud APIs instead, explicitly targeting $100B+ in combined cloud and AI revenue. Zhipu AI is making the same pivot simultaneously, releasing GLM-5-Turbo as a cloud-only model.

**GPT-4o is fully retired today as OpenAI completes its consolidation around the GPT-5.x family.** April 3 is the deadline OpenAI set for GPT-4o to be retired from all ChatGPT plans and APIs. The current lineup consolidates around GPT-5.4 standard, GPT-5.4 Thinking (deep reasoning), GPT-5.4 Pro (maximum capability), and GPT-5.4 mini (cost-efficient). GPT-5.4 is 33% less likely to make errors in individual claims versus GPT-5.2. Any developer with hard-coded GPT-4o API calls now faces broken integrations.

**Today's Signal:** Three of today's four main stories are about AI trust — and they pull in different directions. The Perplexity lawsuit is the clearest illustration yet that AI privacy practices are facing the same class-action scrutiny that targeted social media a decade ago; the timing is especially damaging because Perplexity is trying to expand into products that require handing over even more data. Alibaba's closed-source pivot shows the other trust axis: developer trust as a commercial lever, extended when building ecosystem mindshare and withdrawn once platform lock-in is sufficient. The Rowhammer GPU attacks reveal a third and underappreciated trust gap: shared cloud AI infrastructure carries a hardware-level attack surface that is categorically different from software vulnerabilities.

## Notable Model Releases

**Google releases Gemma 4 under Apache 2.0 — the first fully open-source Gemma models, built from Gemini 3 research.** Announced April 2 in four sizes — E2B and E4B (edge devices, mobile phones, Raspberry Pi), 26B Mixture-of-Experts (MoE), and 31B — Gemma 4 marks a significant licensing shift: prior Gemma generations were open-weight under a custom permissive license; Gemma 4 carries the OSI-approved Apache 2.0 license, enabling commercial bundling and deployment without legal friction. Benchmark performance for the 31B model: 89.2% on AIME 2026 (advanced mathematics), 84.3% on GPQA Diamond (scientific knowledge), 80% on LiveCodeBench v6 (competitive coding), 86.4% on τ2-bench (agentic tool use). The model supports 140+ languages and context windows up to 256K tokens. Day-one support across Hugging Face, Ollama, Kaggle, llama.cpp, MLX, and Google AI Studio. The Gemma family has been downloaded over 400 million times across all versions, generating more than 100,000 community variants.

**Today's Signal:** Gemma 4's Apache 2.0 switch is the more significant move than the benchmark numbers. Prior Gemma licensing created legal friction for enterprises and startups building products on the models; Apache 2.0 removes that entirely. Combined with benchmarks that now compete with proprietary frontier models at the 31B scale, this materially expands the addressable market for production deployments of open models — and lands precisely as Alibaba closes the same door on its own open models. The simultaneous open/closed divergence between Google and Alibaba reflects a fundamental disagreement about where value is captured in the AI stack.

**Product Hunt top launches (April 2):** April 3 daily leaderboard not yet available. April 2 top launches: MuleRun (#1, 544 upvotes) — an AI that builds persistent context across a user's workflows over time, learning how they work rather than resetting each session; Glam AI (#2, 484 upvotes) — AI-powered trend photo and video content creation for social media; GLM-5-Turbo (#3, 300 upvotes) — Zhipu AI's high-speed agentic model explicitly designed for the OpenClaw ecosystem (an open-source alternative to Claude Code), positioning as the inference backbone for third-party coding agents.

No major standalone funding rounds announced today.

**Today's Signal:** The Product Hunt leaderboard shows two competing product philosophies with no sign of convergence. MuleRun's continuous-learning model bets that users will pay for an AI that accumulates context over time — a lock-in thesis. GLM-5-Turbo's positioning as an OpenClaw-native inference layer bets on commoditization: that the agent harness matters more than the model, and that cheap, fast models win by routing. Both strategies are rational responses to the same market; which one compounds better over the next 18 months is the open question in developer tooling.

**"Decisions that eroded trust in Azure" by former Azure Core engineer Axel Rietschin lands on Hacker News with 869 points in under 24 hours.** Rietschin, who contributed to foundational Azure technologies including the Azure Boost offload card and the Windows Container platform (which powers Docker, AKS, and Windows Sandbox), begins a multi-part series alleging that internal complacency and strategic errors at Microsoft Azure nearly cost the company its largest customer — OpenAI — and damaged its relationship with the US government. The opening installment describes the failures as "one of the silliest, most preventable, and most costly mishaps of the 21st century" and claims the combined effect "vaporized" trillions in value. Specific technical failures are promised in subsequent posts. The HN thread is unusually candid, with former Azure engineers adding anecdotes about consistency issues, race conditions, and organizational dysfunction at scale. One commenter: "In my experience Azure is full of consistency issues and race conditions."

**Today's Signal:** The Azure post lands in the same week Microsoft launched its first in-house AI models (the MAI family), reinforcing that the company can build frontier AI. This insider account raises the pointed counterquestion: whether the cloud infrastructure those models depend on has been managed with equivalent rigor. The 869-point score — exceptionally high for a criticism of a major cloud provider — suggests the post is resonating well beyond the usual Microsoft-skeptic audience. If subsequent technical installments are as specific as the opening implies, this could become one of the more consequential public critiques of a major cloud platform in recent memory, and puts pressure on Microsoft to respond in detail.
