# AI Intelligence Briefing — 2026-04-10

## Top News

**CoreWeave strikes a new cloud deal with Anthropic one day after the $21B Meta agreement.** Reuters reported Friday that CoreWeave has signed a multi-year agreement with Anthropic to supply cloud computing capacity for the Claude family of models, sending CoreWeave shares up more than 5% in premarket trading. Financial terms were not disclosed. The deal's initial focus is a phased infrastructure rollout with potential to expand. It arrives one day after CoreWeave's $21B agreement with Meta (covered yesterday) and continues a pattern of CoreWeave rapidly diversifying its customer base: Microsoft accounted for 67% of revenue last year; it is now building major positions with OpenAI ($11.9B deal), Meta, and now Anthropic — meaning CoreWeave holds compute agreements with all four major frontier AI labs. For Anthropic, the deal addresses a structural constraint: the company has simultaneously committed to scaling access to Claude (including through Project Glasswing's $100M credit pool) while operating under its Pentagon blacklisting, which narrows the pool of U.S. government cloud infrastructure it can access.

**Meta AI app climbs from #57 to #5 on the U.S. App Store following the Muse Spark launch, but the app's social exposure mechanics are generating backlash.** TechCrunch reported Friday that the Meta AI app has seen a sharp spike in downloads since Wednesday's Muse Spark debut, per Appfigures data. Alongside the numbers, TechCrunch published a detailed account of the app's social visibility behavior: when a user downloads or joins the Meta AI app, Instagram sends a notification to their contacts announcing it — without requesting permission, and without a disclosed opt-out. AI conversations within the app are also used for ad targeting. The app has had this behavior since its launch in April 2025; the surge in downloads after Muse Spark has now made the notification visible to a much wider audience. The issue connects to a documented pattern: Meta removed a "Discover" feed in mid-2025 after users accidentally made private conversations public; the current notification system is a lower-severity version of the same disclosure-by-default design.

**Microsoft silently suspended Windows driver-signing accounts used by WireGuard, VeraCrypt, MemTest86, and Windscribe — then became unreachable for weeks.** TechCrunch reported April 8 that Microsoft automatically suspended Windows Hardware Program accounts belonging to maintainers of four high-profile open source security tools, without prior notification to the developers and with no functioning human appeal process. WireGuard maintainer Jason A. Donenfeld: "what if there were some critical [remote code execution vulnerability] in WireGuard, being exploited in the wild, and I needed to update users immediately?" VeraCrypt developer Mounir Idrassi reported trying to reach Microsoft through multiple channels for weeks before receiving only automated replies. Microsoft VP Scott Hanselman acknowledged the issue after TechCrunch's reporting went viral, explaining the accounts were suspended for failing a mandatory partner verification process announced in October 2025 — but the developers say they received no warning of the suspension itself. Microsoft EVP Pavan Davuluri said the company would "review how we communicate changes like this." The story is trending on Hacker News today as security engineers debate what it reveals about Microsoft's support infrastructure for the open-source ecosystem that runs on Windows.

**Today's Signal:** Two of today's three main stories involve trust at the infrastructure layer: Microsoft's silent suspension of critical security tool maintainers and Meta's undisclosed social notification behavior both represent the same pattern — large platforms treating user data and developer relationships as default-on, disclosure-by-default systems until external pressure forces a change. The CoreWeave/Anthropic deal, by contrast, is unambiguously positive infrastructure news: it suggests Anthropic has negotiated compute capacity it needs to scale Claude independently of government contract access. If Anthropic's IPO materializes in late 2026 as expected, today's deal strengthens the "we have the infrastructure to operate at scale" part of the pitch.

## Funding & Product News

**GitButler raises $17M Series A from a16z to build "what comes after Git."** The company — founded by Scott Chacon, one of GitHub's original co-founders — announced a $17M Series A led by a16z, with participation from Fly Ventures and A Capital. GitButler's core argument is that Git was designed around individual developers committing discrete human-written changes, and that AI-assisted development — where agents generate thousands of file edits in a single session, operate in parallel across multiple branches, and produce changes that exceed a human's cognitive review capacity — breaks Git's underlying assumptions. The HN thread (174 points, 379 comments) is running live: some commenters are skeptical that the problem requires a new version control system rather than better tooling on top of Git; others argue that merge conflict resolution alone is already a significant AI bottleneck. Peter Levine, who joins the board, worked with Chacon previously on GitHub's board.

**Snap's new Specs unit signs a multi-year Qualcomm chip deal for AI smart glasses.** Reuters reported Friday that Specs — the standalone Snap subsidiary formed in January to compete with Meta's Ray-Ban smart glasses — has announced a multi-year agreement with Qualcomm for the chips that will power its upcoming devices. This is Specs' first major public move since its formation. Snap structured Specs as a standalone unit explicitly to enable external investment and partnership flexibility not available inside Snapchat's main corporate structure.

**Today's Signal:** The GitButler raise is the structurally interesting story. a16z leading a $17M Series A for a company arguing that AI changes what version control fundamentally needs to do is a direct bet on developer infrastructure obsolescence — not just AI tooling layered on top of existing infrastructure. The framing that Git's assumptions break under agentic development is the same thesis behind tools like SWE-bench harnesses and multi-agent coding environments. Whether the problem requires replacing Git or augmenting it is genuinely open, and 379 HN comments suggests this question has real practitioner stakes.

## Discussions & Debates

**"I still prefer MCP over skills" lands on Hacker News with 244 points and 199 comments, opening the MCP-vs.-Skills infrastructure debate.** Developer David Mohl's post argues that MCP (Model Context Protocol) is the right architecture for giving LLMs access to services, while "Skills" — Markdown files dropped in a repository that teach an LLM how to use a CLI or tool — are appropriate only for pure knowledge transfer. His core objection: Skills that require a CLI to do anything fail in every AI client that can't execute binaries (ChatGPT's web interface, standard Claude, Perplexity). MCP, by contrast, is an API abstraction: it handles auth via OAuth, requires no local installation for remote servers, and works from any MCP-capable client on any device. He frames the dichotomy as "Connectors vs. Manuals": MCPs connect LLMs to services; Skills should only teach LLMs about tools they already have. The HN thread runs the expected debate — several commenters defend Skills' simplicity for small integrations; others agree with Mohl that the industry is at risk of standardizing around a worse abstraction. The discussion is gaining traction the same day that MCP crossed 97 million cumulative installs.

**ETH Zurich's 17,000-qubit paper in Nature triggers a Hacker News discussion (129 points, 28 comments) about what quantum milestones actually mean.** The paper, published April 8, demonstrates a geometric-phase swap gate for neutral-atom qubits that operates at 99.91% fidelity simultaneously on 17,000 qubit pairs. The HN title ("ETH Zurich demonstrates 17,000 qubit array with 99.91% fidelity") attracted immediate correction from commenters: the 17,000 pairs are not independently controllable qubits in a general-purpose programmable computer; they operated in parallel inside an optical lattice without cross-pair interactions. The paper's actual contribution is a highly noise-resistant gate mechanism based on geometric phases — a meaningful step toward fault-tolerant neutral-atom quantum computing, but not evidence of a 17,000-qubit general quantum computer. The thread's meta-argument: AI model hype has raised tolerance for overstated capability claims across adjacent fields, and quantum computing announcement culture has followed suit.

**Today's Signal:** Both threads are pressure-testing standards: the MCP/Skills thread is practitioners arguing about which infrastructure abstraction will win for agentic AI (a real question with large downstream implications for developer lock-in and ecosystem fragmentation). The ETH Zurich thread is the technical community doing the same work on quantum computing that it did on the Walmart conversion data and Steve Krouse's vibe coding critique this week — demanding that specific capability claims match the actual experimental design. The pattern across this week's discussions is consistent: calibration, not enthusiasm, is what practitioners want to talk about right now.
