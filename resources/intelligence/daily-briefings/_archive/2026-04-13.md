# AI Intelligence Briefing — 2026-04-13

## Top News

**Someone threw a Molotov cocktail at Sam Altman's San Francisco home; Altman responded in a blog post acknowledging he is a "flawed person."** Early Friday morning (April 10), 20-year-old Daniel Alejandro Moreno-Gama allegedly threw a Molotov cocktail at Altman's home in Russian Hill, hitting an exterior gate. No one was hurt; Altman and his family were asleep. The suspect was later arrested at OpenAI headquarters, where he was threatening to burn down the building. In a Friday evening blog post, Altman called the preceding New Yorker article "incendiary" and said it had arrived "at a time of great anxiety about AI" in ways he had underestimated. He acknowledged conflict avoidance as a core personal mistake, said "I am a flawed person in the center of an exceptionally complex situation," and apologized to people he had hurt. The attack arrived three days after the April 6 New Yorker profile (covered in the April 7 briefing) raised pointed questions about his trustworthiness — and Altman drew an explicit causal connection in the post.

**Forensic analysis of 119,866 Claude Code API calls shows Anthropic silently downgraded prompt cache TTL from 1 hour to 5 minutes in early March, causing 17–26% cost inflation for heavy users.** GitHub issue #46829, filed April 12 and trending on Hacker News with 518 points and 397 comments, presents a day-by-day breakdown showing that Anthropic defaulted to 1-hour cache TTL (time-to-live) throughout February 2026, then silently reverted to 5-minute TTL around March 6–8. The practical consequence: any pause longer than 5 minutes in a coding session causes the entire cached context to expire and re-upload at 12.5× the cost of a cache read, inflating monthly API costs by 25% or more for users with long, high-context sessions. Subscription users who began hitting their 5-hour quota limits for the first time in March now have a likely explanation. Anthropic labeled the issue "Closed As Not Planned" without confirming or denying whether the TTL change was intentional. This is structurally distinct from GitHub issue #42796 (covered in the April 7 briefing): that issue was about reasoning quality degradation; this is about infrastructure-level billing behavior.

**France has announced it will migrate government computers from Windows to Linux, explicitly citing digital sovereignty concerns under the Trump administration.** TechCrunch reported April 10 that the French government will begin the migration at DINUM (Direction Interministérielle du Numérique — France's government digital agency), with no distribution named and no specific timeline disclosed. The move follows France's January announcement that it would stop using Microsoft Teams in favor of French-made Visio. French officials cited an accelerating pattern of U.S. government unpredictability — including the weaponization of sanctions against ICC (International Criminal Court) judges that terminated their access to U.S. bank accounts and tech services — as the structural reason for reducing dependency on American platforms. The announcement is not AI-specific, but it is AI-relevant: governments migrating off Microsoft cloud infrastructure reduce the default lock-in that makes U.S. AI services the standard for government-scale deployments.

**Today's Signal:** All three stories converge on the same theme: the AI industry's acceleration is generating backlash at multiple layers — physical, financial, and geopolitical — faster than either the labs or governments anticipated. The Altman attack represents the individual/consumer layer; the hidden cache TTL change represents the developer-trust layer; France's migration represents the nation-state sovereignty layer. The common thread is that disclosure gaps — what AI companies don't say publicly, and when — are becoming structural rather than incidental.

## Funding & Product News

**OpenAI silently removed Study Mode from ChatGPT.** A "Tell HN" post filed approximately 21 hours ago — 170 points, 72 comments — confirmed that OpenAI has quietly removed Study Mode, a feature that was advertised at the GPT-5 rollout and used heavily for language learning and academic study. No announcement or explanation was provided. Commenters in the thread noted that OpenAI also recently removed the "nerdy" persona option and speculated that Study Mode may have been removed because its underlying system prompt triggered OpenAI's own guardrails for "robotic" or persona-modifying prompts. The removal fits a broader product pattern: OpenAI is consolidating ChatGPT toward a single unified interface rather than per-session persona modes, in apparent preparation for the super-app architecture expected with the GPT-5.5/Spud rollout.

**Today's Signal:** A quiet weekend with no major launches or funding rounds. The Study Mode removal is a minor product story but signals a feature-pruning philosophy that may frustrate ChatGPT's educational user base — historically one of the product's strongest organic growth verticals. As OpenAI converges toward the Spud super-app architecture, expect continued deprecation of specialized modes that don't fit the unified interface vision.

## Discussions & Debates

**Alberto Romero's "AI Will Be Met with Violence, and Nothing Good Will Come of It" lands on Hacker News with 338 points and 598 comments, directly connecting the Altman attack to Luddite history.** Published April 11 on The Algorithmic Bridge, the essay draws a direct parallel between April 1812 — when mill owner William Horsfall was shot by Luddite George Mellor — and April 2026, casting Altman as the "datacenter owner" whose rhetoric parallels Horsfall's boasts about riding in Luddite blood. Romero's central argument: as the objects of AI-related anxiety become increasingly unreachable behind fences, algorithms, and wealth, people will direct violence toward human targets. He writes: "If people feel that they have no place in the future... then it's die or kill." The HN thread (flagged from the front page but still active with 598 comments) splits between those who frame the violence as inevitable class-conflict escalation, those who argue AI is being used as a scapegoat for pre-existing inequality, and those who note that the HN developer community is now expressing these concerns precisely because they are the ones building the technology.

**Bryan Cantrill's "The Peril of Laziness Lost" lands on HN with 414 points and 136 comments, arguing that LLMs undermine the design virtue that makes software good.** Published April 12 on Cantrill's blog, the essay builds on Larry Wall's "three virtues of a programmer" (laziness, impatience, hubris) to argue that programmer laziness — the drive toward elegant abstractions that do more with less, at the cost of upfront effort — is the most profound of the three. Cantrill's thesis: "LLMs inherently lack the virtue of laziness. Work costs nothing to an LLM. LLMs do not feel a need to optimize for their own (or anyone's) future time." He argues that AI-generated code optimizes for perverse vanity metrics — apparent completeness, line count — rather than conceptual simplicity, making systems larger rather than better. His prescription: use LLMs to address explicit technical debt and enforce existing human-designed abstractions, but resist letting them set those abstractions. The HN thread engages substantively with the thesis.

**Today's Signal:** Both threads point at the same structural problem from different directions. Romero's essay is about what happens socially when people feel excluded from the AI future being built around them. Cantrill's essay is about what happens technically when the tool building that future doesn't share the human constraint — finite time and cognitive load — that has historically produced good software. Both arguments converge on the same diagnosis: when the costs of AI-generated output are externalized — onto other people, future maintainers, or the social fabric — the incentive to build well disappears.
