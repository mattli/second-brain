# AI Intelligence Briefing — 2026-04-16

## Top News

**Google handed user data to ICE without notification, EFF files complaints with state attorneys general.** A Ph.D. student who briefly attended a pro-Palestinian protest in 2024 has published an account of how Google surrendered his account data to Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) via administrative subpoena — without ever notifying him, breaking a nearly decade-long company promise to alert users before complying with law enforcement requests. The Electronic Frontier Foundation (EFF) filed complaints with California and New York attorneys general today, asking them to investigate Google for deceptive trade practices. The EFF story drew over 1,500 points on Hacker News, making it one of the most-discussed tech stories of the week.

**Snap lays off approximately 1,000 employees — 16% of its workforce — citing AI.** CEO Evan Spiegel sent a letter to staff attributing the cuts in part to artificial intelligence reducing repetitive work, joining a broader wave of tech companies reducing headcount as AI automates routine tasks. The company is also closing more than 300 open roles.

**U.S. court rules AI conversation logs do not carry attorney-client privilege.** In *US v. Heppner* (S.D.N.Y. 2026), Judge Rakoff ruled that communications with AI tools are not protected by attorney-client privilege, a ruling with significant implications for how legal teams can use AI assistants without inadvertently exposing privileged strategy.

**Federal regulators move to require data centers to disclose energy consumption.** The U.S. Energy Information Administration (EIA) told two U.S. senators it plans to make data center energy reporting mandatory nationwide. Two regional pilot surveys are currently underway in Texas, Virginia, and Washington state; the mandatory national survey is expected to launch after September. The move follows bipartisan pressure from Sens. Josh Hawley and Elizabeth Warren.

**AI-driven cybersecurity capabilities are forcing a rethink of the security industry's fundamentals.** A widely circulated essay on Hacker News argued that Anthropic's recently previewed Claude Mythos model — evaluated by the U.K.'s AI Security Institute (AISI) — can complete a simulated 32-step corporate network attack in roughly 3 of 10 attempts at a cost of ~$12,500 per run, with no signs of diminishing returns at higher token budgets. The analysis concludes that hardening a system now requires spending more tokens finding exploits than attackers spend exploiting them — effectively a "proof of work" arms race determined by budget rather than skill.

**Today's Signal:** The Google/ICE story and the Snap layoffs together illustrate two accelerating pressures: AI is reshaping how governments can surveil individuals via corporate data, and it is reshaping corporate workforce calculus at the same time. The week is arriving at the intersection of AI capability, civil liberties, and economic disruption — and the legal system is clearly not yet caught up.

## Notable Model Releases

**Anthropic Claude Mythos Preview** (previewed ~April 7; AISI third-party evaluation published ~April 13). Anthropic released a preview of Claude Mythos, a general-purpose model with especially strong cybersecurity capabilities. The U.K. AI Security Institute's independent evaluation found Mythos "a step up over previous frontier models" and the only model tested capable of completing a 32-step simulated corporate network attack end-to-end. In response, Anthropic launched Project Glasswing, an initiative to proactively use Mythos to help secure critical software before attackers can exploit similar capabilities. The model is currently in limited preview.

**Today's Signal:** Mythos marks a threshold where AI can autonomously chain together complex exploitation steps — not just assist with individual tasks. If Anthropic is right that this capability will be matched and exceeded quickly, the security industry's assumption that human expertise is the scarce resource is about to be overturned by compute budgets.

## Funding & Product News

**Canva launched Canva AI 2.0 today at its annual Canva Create event in Los Angeles**, calling it "the biggest evolution in its history." The release transforms Canva from a design tool into a conversational, agentic platform: users describe what they want and an orchestration layer pulls together the full suite to produce finished, editable outputs. New capabilities include persistent memory (personalized to each user's style), web research on-demand, multi-app connectors (Slack, Gmail, Google Drive, Zoom, Notion), and scheduled autonomous tasks. Canva claims its proprietary models are up to 7x faster and 30x cheaper than comparable frontier alternatives. A research preview is rolling out today to the first 1 million users. Canva also disclosed it is now the third most-used AI platform in the world per Andreessen Horowitz research.

**Adobe launched Firefly AI Assistant**, the commercial release of its previously teased "Project Moonlight." The assistant orchestrates tasks across the full Creative Cloud suite — Photoshop, Premiere, Lightroom, Illustrator, Express — and can execute multi-step workflows with user-facing controls like sliders and buttons. Adobe says the assistant learns user style preferences over time. Public beta is coming in the next few weeks; pricing was not disclosed.

**OpenAI launched ChatGPT for Excel (beta)**, a native Excel add-in that lets users build full spreadsheets, query data across tabs, fix formulas, and update workbooks in real time via natural language. Available now for Business, Enterprise, Edu, Pro, and Plus users globally (excluding EU).

**Google released a native Gemini app for macOS**, giving Gemini a dedicated desktop presence that rivals OpenAI and Anthropic's existing Mac apps. The app supports an Option+Space global shortcut, screen sharing for in-context help, image generation (Nano Banana), and video generation (Veo). Available on macOS 15+ globally.

**Anthropic is declining preemptive funding offers at an $800B+ valuation**, according to Bloomberg, though it hasn't ruled out future fundraising. VCs are keen: Anthropic's annualized revenue reportedly hit $30 billion by end of March, up from $9 billion at year-end 2025. The company's last formal round closed in February at a $380B valuation.

**Accel raised a $5 billion fund** for late-stage bets, signaling continued appetite for large growth-stage investments even as some macro uncertainty persists.

**Today's Signal:** Canva AI 2.0 and Adobe Firefly AI Assistant mark a maturation point for AI in creative tooling — the race is shifting from point features (AI-generate an image) to system-level agentic platforms (AI orchestrates your entire workflow). The incumbents are building moats through integration breadth rather than model capability alone.

## Discussions & Debates

**Cal.com announced it is going closed source, explicitly blaming AI-powered vulnerability scanning.** The scheduling platform, which has been open source for five years, published a blog post explaining that AI tools can now be pointed at a public codebase to systematically find and exploit vulnerabilities — effectively handing attackers "the blueprints to the vault." The company is releasing a community version (Cal.diy) under MIT license but moving its production codebase private. The post drew 324 points on Hacker News and a heated thread debating whether this rationale is legitimate or an overreaction, with some arguing that security through obscurity is not real security and others acknowledging that AI-enabled attack automation has genuinely shifted the threat model for open source projects.

**The Codex-hacked-a-Samsung-TV writeup showed AI autonomously completing a hardware privilege escalation from a limited browser shell to root.** Security researchers gave OpenAI's Codex a foothold inside a Samsung TV's browser application and asked it to escalate to root without providing any specific exploit path. Codex audited vendor kernel source code, identified a world-writable physical memory interface, built and deployed exploits against the live device, and adapted when constraints blocked its first approaches — ultimately achieving root. Researchers noted that the next obvious step (and "slightly concerning" one) would be letting AI attempt the initial breach as well.

**Today's Signal:** Both discussions converge on the same shift: AI isn't just a tool for finding bugs faster — it's changing the economics of the attacker/defender relationship at a fundamental level. The Cal.com debate reflects genuine anxiety about what open source means in a world where any codebase is now a continuous attack surface for autonomous agents.
