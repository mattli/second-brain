# AI Intelligence Briefing — 2026-05-01

## Top News

**Elon Musk admitted under oath that xAI used model distillation from OpenAI models to help train Grok.** Testifying in California federal court on April 30, Musk was asked whether xAI had applied distillation techniques to OpenAI's outputs; he described it as a "general practice" in the industry and confirmed xAI did it "partly." The disclosure is legally awkward: Musk's lawsuit is premised on the argument that OpenAI's intellectual property belongs to humanity, and his own company appears to have leaned on OpenAI's model outputs to bootstrap a competitor. In separate testimony, Musk ranked global AI providers as: Anthropic first, OpenAI second, Google third, then Chinese open source — and characterized xAI as "a much smaller company with just a few hundred employees."

**The UK's AI Security Institute (AISI) published an evaluation finding GPT-5.5 can complete a multi-step corporate network attack end-to-end, making it the second model to clear this bar after Claude Mythos Preview.** The evaluation, published April 30, used AISI's "The Last Ones" simulation — a 32-step corporate network infiltration exercise estimated to take a human roughly 20 hours. GPT-5.5 succeeded in 2 of 10 attempts; Mythos succeeded in 3 of 10. AISI notes that performance scales with token budget and hasn't plateaued, and concludes this is "a broader trend" rather than a Mythos-specific anomaly. GPT-5.5 also matched Mythos on isolated expert capture-the-flag tasks, nearly tying at the hardest difficulty level.

**Anthropic's $50 billion fundraise accelerated significantly: investors were given a 48-hour allocation deadline and the round is now expected to close within two weeks.** Reported May 1 by multiple outlets citing sources familiar with the matter, the compressed timeline reflects how oversubscribed the round is at a ~$900 billion valuation — the same round that as recently as last week was pending a May board meeting decision. The urgency pushes what was expected to be Anthropic's final private raise toward a near-term close.

**The Mini Shai-Hulud supply chain campaign compromised the PyTorch Lightning Python library, with the attack now spanning multiple package registries.** Versions 2.6.2 and 2.6.3 of the `lightning` PyPI package, published April 30, contain malware that executes on import, stealing developer credentials, GitHub tokens, cloud secrets, and cryptocurrency wallets, then attempts to poison GitHub repositories. Semgrep and Aikido Security published advisories April 30; developer-tech.com reported May 1 that the same campaign has since spread to npm, PHP Packagist, RubyGems, and Go modules. PyTorch Lightning sits in the dependency tree of machine learning training workflows at enterprise scale, giving attackers direct access to high-performance computing clusters and cloud storage where training data and credentials are stored.

**Today's Signal:** The AISI GPT-5.5 result confirms that autonomous offensive cyber capability at a meaningful level is no longer a one-model anomaly — it's a replicable result across labs. Combined with the PyTorch Lightning supply chain attack targeting AI training environments specifically, this is a day where AI infrastructure risk moved from theoretical to operational on two fronts simultaneously. The Musk distillation admission, meanwhile, inadvertently clarifies a competitive dynamic that has been poorly understood: even the loudest critic of OpenAI was using its models as a training signal.

## Notable Model Releases

**xAI released Grok 4.3 on April 30**, now listed on OpenRouter at $1.25 per million input tokens and $2.50 per million output — approximately 37% lower input and 58% lower output pricing than Grok 4.20. New capabilities include native video input (Grok 4.20 was image-only), direct generation of downloadable PDFs, spreadsheets, and presentation decks, and tighter integration with Grok Computer, xAI's desktop automation agent. The 1 million token context window is retained. Reasoning is always active and cannot be disabled. Artificial Analysis rates Grok 4.3 at 53 on its Intelligence Index, with the biggest single gain on instruction-following benchmarks; Grok 4.20 still leads on factual accuracy and non-hallucination rate. The release landed on Hacker News with 72 points and 81 comments.

**Today's Signal:** Grok 4.3's significant price cuts while retaining the largest context window of any Western closed model represents the most aggressive cost-efficiency move from the xAI/SpaceX platform since the Cursor partnership was announced. The combination of lower API prices, video input, and native document generation positions Grok 4.3 as a more direct competitor to Claude and GPT-5.5 for enterprise workflows — but the persistent absence of cross-session memory remains a meaningful gap at $300/month.

## Funding & Product News

**Palo Alto Networks announced it will acquire Portkey, a startup that provides AI gateway infrastructure for securing and observing AI agent workflows.** Announced April 30, Portkey provides access controls, rate limiting, budget enforcement, logging, and observability across AI agent traffic — the kind of tooling that ensures agents don't overspend, overreach, or exfiltrate sensitive data. Palo Alto described the acquisition as addressing "the rise of AI agents" as an attack surface. No financial terms were disclosed. Portkey had been the most visible independent vendor in the AI agent security gateway category.

**Today's Signal:** The Portkey acquisition confirms that AI agent access control has crossed from startup niche into M&A target. Major security incumbents now treat observability and policy enforcement for AI agents — who can call what, at what cost, with what scope — as standard product infrastructure rather than an optional add-on. For builders, this signals the "bolt it on later" phase for AI agent security is closing.

## Discussions & Debates

**"Opus 4.7 knows the real Kelsey" drew 356 points and 183 comments on Hacker News today.** The piece, by Kelsey Piper at Vox's Future Perfect, documents a series of deanonymization tests run against Claude Opus 4.7. From 125 words of an unpublished political column, pasted in incognito mode with no login, Opus 4.7 returned her name. The model also identified her from a school progress report about a student's Pokémon essays, from a movie review she'd never published, and from a 15-year-old college application essay. ChatGPT guessed wrong; Gemini guessed wrong; Opus 4.7 succeeded repeatedly. Piper concludes that anyone who has written prolifically under their real name has likely lost meaningful anonymity. The model failed to identify friends with minimal online presence — but did guess close mutual friends from the same social circle, suggesting stylistic fingerprints spread through writing communities.

**Today's Signal:** The Kelsey Piper result illustrates a structural asymmetry in model quality improvements: the same capability gains that make Opus 4.7 more useful for open-ended tasks also make it better at stylometric identification — with no separate investment in surveillance capability. The HN discussion, following AISI's cyber evaluation report published the same day, concentrated on the same underlying insight: frontier model improvements don't have on/off switches for dual-use effects.
