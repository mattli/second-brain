# AI Intelligence Briefing — 2026-05-07

## Top News

**Anthropic announced a deal to take over SpaceX's entire Colossus 1 data center in Memphis — 220,000+ Nvidia GPUs, 300 MW capacity — at its "Code with Claude" developer conference on May 6.** Reuters and the New York Times reported the agreement May 6–7. CEO Dario Amodei disclosed at the event that Anthropic grew 80x in annualized revenue in Q1 against an internal plan of 10x, calling it "crazy and too hard to handle." He also confirmed that Elon Musk — who last month publicly called Anthropic "evil" and "misanthropic" — visited the company last week, concluded "no one set off my evil detector," and agreed to the Colossus lease. Alongside the infrastructure news, Anthropic doubled Claude Code rate limits for Pro and Max plans and removed peak-hour caps. The company also previewed "Dreaming," a research feature that lets AI agents self-improve between sessions by reviewing their prior work and updating their own preference files. The Colossus deal is notable not only for scale — it's the largest single-site GPU commitment Anthropic has disclosed — but because it involves a facility built by a company whose CEO publicly attacked Anthropic as recently as last month.

**Samsung crossed $1 trillion in market capitalization on May 6, becoming only the second Asian company after TSMC to reach the milestone.** Shares surged 10–15% in a single session, driven by AI memory chip demand — specifically high-bandwidth memory (HBM), where Samsung and SK Hynix are the dominant suppliers. Samsung's stock has roughly quadrupled over the past year as AI training and inference clusters consumed HBM at a rate that has outpaced supply. SK Hynix and TSMC also hit record highs this month. Bloomberg and CNBC reported separately that Apple is in exploratory talks with Samsung about manufacturing iPhone processors on US soil — a geographic diversification play that would be a significant departure from Apple's existing TSMC-primary fab strategy.

**SpaceX filed formal county documents in Grimes County, Texas proposing a $55 billion semiconductor and AI computing manufacturing facility called "Terafab," with total investment potentially reaching $119 billion across phases.** Reuters, TechCrunch, and Bloomberg reported the filing May 6. The facility is described as a "vertically integrated semiconductor manufacturing and advanced computing fabrication facility" — a joint venture involving Tesla and Intel — intended to produce AI chips for SpaceX, xAI, Tesla, and satellite programs. Musk has framed the project as existential: "We either build the Terafab or we don't have the chips." SpaceX is also targeting a June IPO at approximately $1.75 trillion valuation. The Terafab filing, arriving the same week Musk leased Colossus 1 to Anthropic, makes Musk simultaneously the largest non-hyperscaler AI infrastructure supplier and a customer of the leading AI lab he was publicly attacking weeks ago.

**DeepSeek, the Chinese AI lab that has never previously sought outside investment, is in talks for its first VC round at a valuation of approximately $45 billion — led by China's state-backed semiconductor fund.** The Financial Times and Bloomberg reported the fundraise May 6, with TechCrunch providing further detail. The lead investor is China's "Big Fund" (China Integrated Circuit Industry Investment Fund), with Tencent and Alibaba also in discussions. The valuation has roughly doubled from the approximately $20 billion figure discussed just weeks ago. The fundraise is motivated by researcher attrition to better-capitalized competitors; shares would be used for employee compensation. A completed deal would be the first known Big Fund investment in a Chinese large language model company, marking an explicit expansion of China's state semiconductor investment strategy from hardware to frontier AI models.

**Today's Signal:** The Anthropic/Colossus deal, the Samsung trillion-dollar crossing, and the SpaceX Terafab filing all arrived on the same day — and each is a different expression of the same underlying constraint: frontier AI needs more compute than current infrastructure can supply, and the organizations closest to that constraint are taking increasingly unconventional steps to resolve it. Musk leasing to Anthropic and filing for Terafab simultaneously is the week's most structurally interesting data point: the people who need the most AI compute are now the same people building it for everyone else.

## Funding & Product News

**Roche agreed to acquire PathAI for $750 million upfront plus up to $300 million in milestone payments, for a total deal value of up to $1.05 billion.** Announced May 7 via Roche press release and reported by Reuters and the Wall Street Journal. PathAI builds AI-powered image management tools and diagnostic algorithms for digital pathology — the analysis of tissue and biopsy slides that underpins cancer diagnosis, clinical trials, and drug development. The deal builds on a five-year collaboration and a deepened 2024 partnership developing AI companion diagnostic algorithms. The acquisition is expected to close in the second half of 2026. The deal continues the pattern of large pharmaceutical companies internalizing AI pathology capabilities rather than licensing them: the diagnostic algorithm layer, once a startup opportunity, is becoming a standard acquisition target for companies that need it across multiple therapeutic programs.

## Discussions & Debates

**"Vibe coding and agentic engineering are getting closer than I'd like" by Simon Willison generated 633 points and 692 comments on Hacker News**, based on a May 6 post on simonwillison.net drawn from a Heavybit podcast appearance. Willison had previously drawn a firm distinction: vibe coding (accepting AI code without review) is fine for personal tools but "grossly irresponsible" for production; agentic engineering (disciplined AI-assisted development by professional engineers who review outputs) is the legitimate production path. His update: the distinction is blurring in his own practice. As Claude Code reliably produces correct code for familiar patterns, he finds himself not reviewing every output — and feels guilt about it. His resolution is framing AI agents like output from a trusted-but-unaccountable engineering team: trusted until bugs appear, then investigated. He names "normalization of deviance" — the erosion of safety habits through repeated unremarkable success — as the structural risk. The HN discussion concentrated on whether trust without accountability mechanisms can hold at scale, and on what this behavioral shift implies for code review processes, security audits, and engineering organizations that haven't codified what "review" means in an AI-assisted context.

**Today's Signal:** Willison's post is more significant than its framing as personal reflection suggests. He was one of the clearest articulators of the vibe-coding-versus-disciplined-engineering distinction, and he's now reporting that the distinction is breaking down under the weight of accumulated reliable outputs. This is precisely the normalization-of-deviance dynamic he names: the habits don't erode through bad decisions, they erode through good ones. The HN discussion's focus on accountability mechanisms rather than on Willison personally suggests the field has internalized the question: it's not whether individuals will slip, it's what institutional structures prevent the slip from compounding.
