# AI Intelligence Briefing — 2026-05-12

## Top News

**General Motors cut approximately 600 salaried IT workers — more than 10% of its IT department — in an explicit skills swap: clearing out workers whose expertise no longer fits and hiring replacements with AI-focused backgrounds.** Bloomberg first reported the layoffs on May 11; TechCrunch confirmed with GM, which described it as "transforming its Information Technology organization to better position the company for the future." The open positions focus specifically on AI-native development, data engineering and analytics, agent and model development, prompt engineering, and new AI workflows. GM's action is structurally distinct from the tech-sector wave of AI-justified cuts (Cloudflare, Coinbase, Freshworks — all covered last week): those companies were eliminating roles made redundant by deployed AI. GM is doing something different — treating its existing IT workforce as the wrong skills inventory and replacing it wholesale. This is one of the first major industrial companies to execute this kind of explicit AI skills swap at scale.

**Daniel Stenberg, the creator and lead maintainer of curl, published a detailed account of Mythos's security scan of the curl codebase — and the results were more modest than Anthropic's framing of Mythos as "dangerously good" at finding vulnerabilities would suggest.** The May 11 post (609 points, 252 comments on Hacker News) reports that Mythos found five issues total, of which one was confirmed as a genuine vulnerability. Stenberg noted: "counted by the volume of issues found, all the previous AI tools we have used have resulted in larger bugfix amounts." The HN discussion concentrated on two competing interpretations: that curl's exceptionally high code quality made it a hard target regardless of tool capability, and that Mythos's limited access (a single scan pass, not an extended iterative engagement) may have constrained results. Comments from practitioners in the thread who have tested Mythos in other contexts report more substantive findings, suggesting the curl result is more about engagement depth and target hardness than model capability. The post functions as a useful calibration point: Mythos's security capabilities appear real, but real-world results are substantially affected by how the tool is engaged and what it is pointed at.

**App teardowns published Monday by Forbes reveal seven hidden model options inside the Gemini Live app, including two models labeled "RC2" (Release Candidate 2) — meaning near-production readiness — with a "Thinking" variant, plus codenames "Nitrogen" and "Capybara."** The hidden model selector appeared in the app behind a server-side flag; a separate analysis by iWeaver AI identified a Gemini Omni video model in staging. Google I/O 2026 is one week away — keynote on May 19 at 10 a.m. PT. The pattern of pre-I/O leaks surfacing confirmed codenames at RC2 stage is consistent with keynote-stage reveals. Separately, Gemini 3.1 Flash-Lite reached general availability on May 8. The cumulative picture: Google I/O 2026 will likely include at minimum an upgraded Gemini Live experience with selectable models, a new video generation product (Omni), and potentially Gemini 3.2 or 3.5. The last three weeks of relative quiet on Google model releases has been pre-I/O staging, not absence of development.

**"I work in Hollywood. Everyone who used to make TV is now training AI," published by Wired, generated HN discussion** documenting how the collapse of scripted TV and film production has redirected a large cohort of creative professionals — writers, researchers, story editors — into AI training data work as primary income. The piece describes a structural transition where creative labor previously directed at entertainment output is now directed at AI model improvement, with training contractors reporting that the volume and quality of gig work has expanded significantly as Hollywood production volumes have fallen. This is a distinct dynamic from AI replacing workers: it is an industry that has already contracted redirecting its displaced workforce into the AI supply chain.

**Today's Signal:** The GM skills swap and the Hollywood training story together describe the labor market consequences of AI adoption from opposite ends: companies replacing general IT skills with AI-native ones, and displaced creative workers being absorbed into the AI training pipeline. Both dynamics are operating at scale today. The Mythos/curl calibration is worth holding onto: frontier model capability is real but context-dependent, and vendor framing ("dangerously good") should be tested against independently reported results. Google I/O next week is the most significant AI event on the calendar for the next 30 days.

## Funding & Product News

**Vapi, an AI voice infrastructure startup, raised a $50 million Series B at approximately $500 million post-money valuation, led by Peak XV Partners, with participation from Microsoft M12, Kleiner Perkins, and Bessemer Venture Partners.** Reported exclusively by TechCrunch on May 12. The round follows a disclosure that Amazon Ring, after evaluating more than 40 AI voice vendors, selected Vapi and now routes 100% of its inbound customer calls through Vapi's platform. Vapi reports processing between 1 million and 5 million calls per day and has handled more than 1 billion total calls across its platform. Enterprise customers include New York Life, Intuit, and Kavak in addition to Ring. The company's architecture is deliberately infrastructure-layer rather than application-layer — selling orchestration tooling and reliability controls to enterprises that want to manage AI agent behavior in live calls, not pre-packaged scripts. Total funding raised to date is $72 million.

**Helsing, the five-year-old European defense AI and drone startup backed by Spotify founder Daniel Ek, is close to raising a new $1.2 billion round at approximately $18 billion valuation, led by Dragoneer and co-led by existing investor Lightspeed.** Reported by the Financial Times and TechCrunch on May 11. The round represents a step up from Helsing's June 2025 raise of €600 million at an estimated €12 billion ($14 billion) valuation. Helsing builds AI targeting and autonomy systems for military drone platforms, with operational deployments in the context of Russia's ongoing war in Ukraine. The round, if confirmed, would place Helsing at roughly 3× the valuation of the next-largest European defense tech startup (Quantum Systems, €3 billion+), and reflects continued institutional conviction that European defense AI is operating at a different demand curve than commercial AI applications.

**Today's Signal:** The Vapi and Helsing raises land on the same day and describe the same structural pattern from different sectors: AI infrastructure that is genuinely load-bearing in production workflows — real calls, real drone targeting — commands a valuation premium over AI products that remain in pilot. The voice AI space has compressed significantly over the past 18 months, and Amazon Ring's "evaluated 40 vendors" disclosure is a useful data point: the market exists, buyers are sophisticated and deliberate, and the winner of a major enterprise deployment translates directly into fundable traction at this scale.

## Discussions & Debates

**"Hardware Attestation as Monopoly Enabler," a Mastodon post by GrapheneOS (the privacy-focused Android fork), became the highest-engagement story on Hacker News today with 1,953 points and 657 comments.** The post argues that Apple's App Attest API and Google's Play Integrity API are gradually expanding hardware-based attestation requirements — convincing an increasing number of services to require certified hardware signatures before granting access — and that over the long term, this will increasingly lock out hardware and OS alternatives that cannot pass platform attestation checks. GrapheneOS has direct skin in this game: it is the primary alternative Android build and has spent years building attestation workarounds that platforms then break. The HN discussion concentrated on the structural argument: hardware attestation was originally framed as an anti-fraud and device-security mechanism, but its expansion to app-level trust decisions effectively makes the platform the gatekeeper for which software is permitted to access services that were previously open. A secondary thread discussed whether regulatory intervention (EU Digital Markets Act, etc.) could reach attestation APIs, given that attestation is framed as a security mechanism rather than a business restriction.

**"Local AI Needs to Be the Norm," published on unix.foo, drew 1,606 points and 632 comments on Hacker News**, arguing that AI inference should run on-device rather than in the cloud by default, using the author's Brutalist Report iOS app (which generates article summaries locally on M-series iPhones) as a worked example. The post's core argument: cloud-based AI inference means every query is observable by the provider, AI availability is dependent on external uptime and pricing decisions, and on-device hardware is now capable enough for a wide range of summarization and classification tasks. The HN discussion split between two camps: those who argue local inference is already practical for many use cases and that the cloud default is a business decision rather than a capability constraint, and those who note that frontier model capabilities still require cloud infrastructure and that the on-device tier covers a narrower range of tasks than the post implies. Several comments pointed to the direct connection with the attestation debate above: if hardware attestation requirements expand further, the platforms that control which AI models can run locally become a new vector for the same monopoly concern.

**Today's Signal:** The hardware attestation and local AI debates are directly linked — both are asking who controls the AI inference layer and under what conditions. The attestation story poses the question at the platform level (can your OS run the software?); the local AI story poses it at the product level (should your AI query leave your device?). Together they describe an emerging second-order policy battleground: not just what AI can do, but where it runs and who can observe it doing so.
