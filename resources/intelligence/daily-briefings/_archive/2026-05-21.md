# AI Intelligence Briefing — 2026-05-21

## Top News

**SpaceX's S-1 filing — the company's first public financial disclosure — revealed that xAI burned $6.4 billion on $3.2 billion in revenue in 2025, up from a $1.56 billion loss on $2.62 billion in 2024, and that SpaceX directed approximately 60% of its 2025 capital expenditure to AI infrastructure, while disclosing that Anthropic is paying xAI $1.25 billion per month through May 2029 for access to Colossus 1 compute.** The S-1 was filed May 20 ahead of a planned SpaceX IPO; The Information, Bloomberg, and TechCrunch reported the xAI financial details the same day. The Anthropic compute contract — worth up to $40+ billion over the contract term if maintained — represents xAI's largest disclosed revenue line and reframes its business model: Colossus was built as a training cluster for Grok, but the S-1 reveals xAI is now functioning as a neocloud, renting capacity to a competitor's frontier lab. Grok has 117 million monthly active users out of 550 million combined users across xAI products. The filing also discloses that xAI plans to scale Grok to "multiple trillions of parameters" and is developing orbital AI data centers planned for 2028. Separately, the S-1 confirms xAI intends to purchase an additional $2.8 billion in gas turbines — the same turbine infrastructure at the center of the NAACP Clean Air Act lawsuit first covered May 14 — while that litigation remains active.

**Today's Signal:** The SpaceX S-1 is the most information-dense AI document published this week. The Anthropic-xAI compute deal is the story inside the story: Anthropic, which competes directly with xAI's Grok, is xAI's anchor customer — paying $1.25B/month for the compute it needs to train Claude. That's a structural dependency that neither company had previously disclosed at that scale. The $2.8B turbine expansion, filed publicly while the NAACP lawsuit is active, signals that xAI is betting on regulatory resolution rather than infrastructure restraint.

**OpenAI is preparing to file IPO paperwork "within days or weeks," targeting a September market debut, working with Goldman Sachs and Morgan Stanley — and in the same 24-hour window, the WSJ and TechCrunch reported that Anthropic is forecasting approximately $10.9 billion in Q2 revenue (130% year-over-year growth) and its first-ever operating profit.** OpenAI IPO timing was confirmed by The Information and Reuters on May 20; Anthropic's financial forecast was reported by WSJ and confirmed by TechCrunch the same day. OpenAI's IPO follows the Musk v. Altman verdict (May 19) that removed the most significant legal cloud on its public offering trajectory; the directed verdict cleared the way without adjudicating the nonprofit conversion questions on the merits. Anthropic's operating profit milestone — the first in the company's history — arrives as it prepares to compete with a newly public OpenAI for enterprise customers. Anthropic's revenue trajectory implies annual revenue north of $40 billion if growth holds through year-end; the company raised at a $61 billion valuation in late 2025.

**Today's Signal:** OpenAI and Anthropic are converging on the same milestone from opposite directions — OpenAI moving from nonprofit origins toward public markets, Anthropic moving from cash-burning startup toward profitability — in the same week. The coincidence is partly structural: both companies are benefiting from the same enterprise AI spending surge that drove Gemini 3.5 Flash's launch pricing and Meta's record Q1 revenue. The divergence is strategic: OpenAI is choosing the public capital markets; Anthropic's first profitable quarter suggests it may not need to.

**An OpenAI reasoning model has disproved the Erdős unit-distance conjecture, an 80-year-old open problem in discrete geometry, by constructing a point configuration that yields n^{1+δ} unit-distance pairs — more than any previously known construction — and the result has been independently verified by external mathematicians including Noga Alon, Melanie Wood, and Thomas Bloom.** Reported by TechCrunch and confirmed on the OpenAI blog (May 20). The conjecture, posed by Paul Erdős in 1946, asked for the maximum number of unit-distance pairs achievable among n points in the plane; the AI-assisted construction exceeds the previously known upper-bound constructions. Verification by Bloom — who was among the mathematicians who publicly debunked OpenAI's false 2025 math claim — carries particular weight as a credibility signal; the participation of Alon and Wood, two of the most prominent combinatorialists working today, confirms the result is not a hallucination or near-miss. The work represents a substantive mathematical contribution, not a benchmark performance — a distinction the AI community has debated since earlier false-positive math announcements.

**Today's Signal:** The Erdős result is notable for what it is and for how it's being received. The same mathematician who caught OpenAI's false 2025 math claim is now among the verifiers of a genuine result — which is the right epistemic process working correctly. The more significant question is whether this is a one-off capability demonstration or the beginning of a systematic pattern: AI systems contributing to open mathematics problems that have resisted human effort for decades. That's a different claim than benchmark leadership.

**Nvidia reported Q4 2025 revenue of $81.6 billion and guided to $91 billion in the current quarter, while Jensen Huang used the earnings call to announce the Vera CPU — a processor purpose-built for agentic AI inference — claiming a $200 billion "brand-new total addressable market" and disclosing $20 billion in Vera CPU sales already this year.** Reported by The Verge, Bloomberg, and CNBC on May 20. Huang's argument: AI agents need to process enormous volumes of tokens with low latency and high throughput — a workload that maps to Vera's architecture better than traditional multi-core CPUs optimized for parallel task execution. Vera is distinct from Nvidia's GPU product line and targets the server CPU market currently dominated by AMD and Intel. The $20 billion in year-to-date sales figure, if accurate, represents Nvidia opening a material second product revenue stream in its first year of CPU availability — a claim that will face scrutiny in the detailed financials.

**Today's Signal:** Huang's framing of Vera as a "brand-new TAM" is a claim that agents create a qualitatively different hardware requirement than both training (GPUs) and traditional compute (conventional CPUs). If the agentic inference bottleneck is real and Vera addresses it, Nvidia is expanding its total addressable market into the $200B+ server CPU segment — a market it has previously ceded entirely to AMD and Intel. The $20B in year-to-date sales is the number to watch in the next quarterly filing.

## Funding & Product News

**Sam Altman offered $2 million in OpenAI tokens — structured as an uncapped SAFE — to every startup in the YC Spring 2026 batch (approximately 169 companies) in exchange for equity, in an announcement made at a YC event on May 20.** Reported by TechCrunch on May 20. The offer is notable for its structure: uncapped SAFEs in exchange for startup equity creates a two-way bet — OpenAI gets equity stakes in the YC cohort; the startups get OpenAI compute and API access at pre-IPO token prices. At 169 startups, the total commitment approaches $340 million in token value if fully subscribed. The offer follows OpenAI's IPO preparations and can be read as both a customer-acquisition strategy (locking YC startups into OpenAI infrastructure at formation) and a pre-IPO asset accumulation (equity in early-stage YC companies has historically been valuable). YC's position as the most influential early-stage accelerator makes this deal structurally different from standard startup programs — it shapes the default AI stack for the next generation of companies at the moment of their founding.

**Today's Signal:** The tokens-for-equity structure is OpenAI's most aggressive distribution play since the enterprise agreement announcements. By targeting YC companies at formation — before they have established infrastructure dependencies — OpenAI is attempting to lock in the developer cohort that will define what "default AI stack" means in 2027 and 2028. The pre-IPO timing is not incidental: startup equity acquired before September would appreciate if the IPO prices above the implied SAFE valuation.

## Discussions & Debates

**The FULU Foundation, a newly formed non-profit, republished the OrcaSlicer-bambulab fork on GitHub after Bambu Lab's legal threats forced original developer Paweł Jarczak to remove it — reaching 443 points and 184 comments on Hacker News today, representing the community's organized response to the AGPL enforcement dispute first reported May 18.** Reported by Tom's Hardware and confirmed on the FULU Foundation GitHub (May 20–21). The FULU Foundation's approach — organizing as a non-profit to republish the fork under the same AGPL-v3 license Bambu itself uses — is designed to harden the legal position: a non-profit entity is harder to silence via cease-and-desist than an individual developer, and the AGPL argument remains the same (Bambu cannot accept the benefits of AGPL-licensed code while using ToS enforcement to prevent the modifications AGPL explicitly authorizes). HN discussion focused on whether non-profit structuring actually changes Bambu's legal options, the Right to Repair angle Louis Rossmann has been funding, and the broader question of whether AGPL's "cloud loophole" argument — cloud back-ends are private infrastructure exempt from AGPL's copyleft requirements — will face a definitive legal test here or be avoided again. The fork's core functionality is restoring direct printer connection that Bambu disabled via its Authorization Control System.

**Today's Signal:** The FULU Foundation's formation is a qualitative escalation from the Geerling essay and Jarczak's individual removal: the open source community has organized into a legal entity specifically to hold the AGPL line against a well-funded manufacturer. The legal question Bambu is avoiding — whether cloud infrastructure can be exempt from AGPL copyleft — is now set up for a direct test. If Bambu sends a cease-and-desist to FULU, FULU has the resources and structure to respond; if it doesn't, the fork survives and the AGPL argument holds in practice even without a court ruling.
