# AI Intelligence Weekly — 2026-W13

> Week of March 23 – March 28, 2026

## Week in Review

The dominant story of Week 13 was governance achieving real legal authority over AI deployment for the first time — Anthropic's preliminary injunction win against the Pentagon established that courts will scrutinize politically retaliatory AI designations on First Amendment grounds — while capital simultaneously doubled down on infrastructure rather than applications, with Kleiner Perkins raising $3.5B "all in on AI" and Shield AI jumping 140% in valuation on a single defense contract. Threading through both was the Cursor/Kimi K2.5 disclosure: a $29.3B coding tool built on an open-weight Chinese model, revealed mid-week, which crystallized a structural question the rest of the week couldn't answer — if open-weight models plus reinforcement learning can match closed frontier models on coding benchmarks, what exactly are the enormous AI product valuations buying?

## AI Context

- **Google TurboQuant (March 24):** Training-free 6x KV (key-value) cache compression, up to 8x speedup on H100 GPUs, zero accuracy loss. If it holds in production, changes the cost economics of long-context workloads for every model — not just Google's. Builders running large-context pipelines should watch the MLX port closely.
- **Claude Code "auto mode" (March 24):** Anthropic shifted the permission decision from human to AI, with a safety layer that checks for risky actions and prompt injection before proceeding. Currently limited to Sonnet 4.6 and Opus 4.6, sandboxed environments recommended. The undisclosed criteria for "risky" is the adoption blocker.
- **OpenAI shuts down Sora (March 25):** The resource reallocation is explicit — shutting down compute-heavy video generation to prioritize coding and reasoning, where Anthropic is winning enterprise contracts. ByteDance is stepping into the consumer video gap in emerging markets (Seedance 2.0, seven markets, US/EU excluded).
- **Mistral Voxtral (March 26):** Open-weight TTS (text-to-speech) model, 3B parameters, voice cloning from under 5 seconds of audio, runs on consumer hardware including smartphones. First serious open alternative to ElevenLabs for on-device voice generation.
- **LangChain/LangGraph CVEs (March 27):** Three critical vulnerabilities in the world's most-used AI application frameworks — path traversal (CVSS 7.5), API key leak via deserialization (CVSS 9.3), SQL injection in LangGraph's checkpoint layer (CVSS 7.3). Patches available; framework downloaded 52M times last week. The AI stack now has a mature attack surface.

## VC Lens

**Kleiner Perkins — $3.5B fund (announced March 24):** KP22 ($1B early-stage) plus KP Select IV ($2.5B growth), framed explicitly as "all in on AI." No forward-looking thesis piece accompanied the raise — the signal is structural: one of the oldest traditional VC firms is repositioning AI as its primary mandate across the full stack, not just the application layer. Combined with Kleiner's recent portfolio activity (Harvey at $11B legal AI, Waymo, Applied Intuition), the pattern is: enterprise AI that can command credibility in regulated or high-stakes domains.

**Sequoia — "Services: The New Software" (Julien Bek, March 5):** Published just before this week, directly relevant to category analysis. Core thesis: the winning AI companies won't sell tools — they'll sell outcomes. The example: a company spends $10K/year on QuickBooks and $120K/year on an accountant; the next legendary company just closes the books. Sequoia distinguishes *intelligence* (writing code, gathering data) from *judgement* (knowing what to build, strategic recommendations) — the thesis is that intelligence is automating, judgement remains human, and the companies that own the output of intelligence at scale will be worth more than the tools. Validates: AI-native services in legal, accounting, compliance, HR. Challenges: any "AI wrapper" product that sells access to intelligence without owning the outcome.

**a16z:** No qualifying thesis piece published this week. Most recent relevant piece ("Your Data Agents Need Context") was March 10–11.

**Notable funding signals:**
- Shield AI raises at $12.7B (up 140% in a year) on a US Air Force autonomous drone contract — capital flowing toward companies that will take the contract terms Anthropic refused.
- Mirage (fka Captions) raises $75M from General Catalyst for AI video — repositioning as an "AI lab" competing with ByteDance's CapCut.
- Nebius: $4.34B convertible debt (March 23); Gimlet Labs: $80M Series A for chip-agnostic inference (March 23) — infrastructure layer, not applications.

## Category Movement

**AI Coding Agents & Developer Tools — crowded, but signals are sharpening.** The product briefing shows minimal traction across ten-plus entries (Synoppy leads at 2 likes). But the week's AI briefings reveal where this category is actually being contested: the Claude Code cheat sheet going viral (458 points on HN) marks the transition from experiment to practiced craft, with layered community knowledge forming. The Cq (Mozilla AI "Stack Overflow for AI agents") discussion mapped the actual failure surface of production AI coding agents — not lack of knowledge, but loss of context over long sessions. The Cursor/Kimi disclosure is the most structurally important signal: a $29.3B product turns out to be 75% RL fine-tuning on an open-weight base, and it *outperforms* frontier closed models on coding benchmarks. Indie "AI coding" tools face a landscape where distribution, user behavior data, and RL on open weights — not model access — are the actual moats.

**AI Agents, Platforms & Infrastructure — one clear signal amid noise.** WURK's 19 likes (agents hire humans for micro-tasks with crypto payments) is the week's traction outlier in this category. The human-in-the-loop angle — where the agent is the buyer and humans are the service — resonated in a way that pure infrastructure pitches (GoPilot, Leap0, Lunar Sandbox) didn't. At the enterprise layer, Databricks entering security with Lakewatch (acquisitions of Antimatter and SiftD.ai alongside the launch) is the real story: a $105B data infrastructure company redrawing its market boundary because AI agent deployment is forcing it to. Indie agent infrastructure builders are attempting to solve the same problem at a different scale.

**AI-Powered Content Creation & Marketing — stagnant at the product level, volatile at the capital level.** The product briefing's most crowded category (11 entries, near-zero traction) is simultaneously seeing the most capital movement: Mirage at $75M, OpenAI exiting video and ByteDance filling the gap. The pattern is consolidation — the application layer is settling into a small number of well-funded players (Mirage, CapCut, Runway) while indie builders continue to produce undifferentiated entries.

**AI Image & Video Generation — effectively abandoned by indie builders this week.** Zero likes across all entries in the product briefing. The real development is at the enterprise/funded layer: Seedance 2.0 in emerging markets, Voxtral for voice. The indie opportunity in this category may be narrowing to highly specific verticals (architectural visualization via IsoRender, Visualizee) rather than general generation.

**Privacy, Security & Encryption — indie category growing in the wrong direction.** Five products in the briefing (Vaul7, PasteLink, ShiftPQC, Dead Man's Switch, Encrypted Clipboard) all at zero traction — while the AI briefings reveal the actual security crisis: LangChain CVEs with CVSS 9.3, Databricks acquiring security companies, a wave of OWASP-class vulnerabilities in AI frameworks. Indie builders are making encryption tools for individual users; the gap they're not addressing is AI-specific security — prompt injection protection, agent sandboxing, agentic access control.

**Builder Community & Networking Maps — notable saturation.** Three products (Builders Map, Builders.to, The Dock) solving the same problem in the same week, all at zero traction. This is a pattern from prior weeks continuing — the idea is easy to build but hits critical mass problems immediately.

## Capital vs. Builder Gaps

**1. Capital → AI security and agent governance; builders → none.**
The biggest gap this week is AI-specific security. The LangChain CVEs (CVSS 9.3 API key leak via prompt injection) landed alongside Databricks acquiring two security companies to build an agentic SIEM. Claude Code auto mode's "safety layer" is Anthropic's own attempt at the same problem. The Anthropic v. Pentagon case is about governance of autonomous AI in high-stakes environments. This is a consistent, week-over-week pattern: every layer of the AI stack is developing serious attack surface, and there is essentially no indie builder activity in AI-specific security tooling — not sandboxing, not prompt injection detection, not agentic access control. The gap exists because the problem is enterprise-grade (requires credibility, compliance expertise, and integration into existing security stacks) and early-stage (the threat model is still being defined). This looks like a genuine opportunity with high barriers to entry — a gap with capital behind it and builders not yet there.

**2. Builders → AI coding tools; capital → distribution, data, and RL.**
The product briefing has ten-plus entries in "AI Coding Agents & Developer Tools," nearly all at zero traction. The Cursor/Kimi disclosure explains why: the actual moat in this category isn't model access — it's distribution (Cursor's 900K+ daily users), proprietary user behavior data, and the ability to do RL on top of open-weight models. Capital is concentrating at the top of this market (Cursor at $29.3B, Claude Code as a platform). Indie builders entering this category are solving a problem where the competitive dynamics favor incumbents structurally. The warning: most of these products are building what Cursor already does, at a fraction of the scale needed to generate the training signal that makes RL fine-tuning work.

**3. Capital (Sequoia thesis) → AI-native services; builders → AI tools.**
Sequoia's "Services" thesis argues the opportunity is in companies that close the books, not sell QuickBooks. This week's indie product landscape is almost entirely tools — not outcome-owning services. The briefing has zero products that take end-to-end ownership of a business outcome. There are legal document explainers (LawIsEasy), not AI that handles legal work. There are invoice generators (InvoiceMama), not AI that manages client finances. The structural gap is real, but it exists partly because AI-native services require trust, accountability, and regulatory exposure that tools avoid. Whether this is an opportunity or a warning depends on whether solo builders can take on the liability that comes with owning the outcome. For most, it's out of scope.

## One Big Question

If Cursor — valued at $29.3B, competing with Claude Code on coding benchmarks — built its product primarily on RL fine-tuning of an open-weight Chinese model rather than a proprietary closed one, and the result outperforms frontier models on the tasks users care about: what does that imply for every other AI product company whose valuation depends on a "proprietary model" narrative? And if the answer is that distribution and user data matter more than model provenance, how many of the current wave of well-funded AI application companies will discover their moats are thinner than their valuations suggest?
