# Internet as Mediating Layer

> TLDR: The internet is now the fundamental layer through which all culture, politics, language, and media is filtered — not a parallel world but the upstream source of real life itself.

## Overview

Erik Torenberg's thesis: the internet is no longer a separate sphere from "real life" — it *is* real life. News, music, movies, politics, and language are all downstream of what happens online first. The normie/online distinction has collapsed; everyone now thinks, speaks, and acts on terms set by internet discourse.

This is not merely a behavioral observation but a structural one. In the same way that mathematics, government, and currency are social technologies that mediate between humans and reality, the internet is the latest and most pervasive such layer. Humans haven't had time to evolve alongside it, which makes it both unavoidable and potentially dangerous.

## Cultural Production Is Now Internet-Native

**Music:** Song structure is being rewritten by TikTok. The verse-chorus-verse-chorus format is no longer required; what's required is a riff that hooks within the first second and works over a 15-second video clip. Just as radio historically constrained song length, TikTok now defines the unit of musical attention. Older bands have gained mass Gen-Z audiences through viral TikTok discovery (e.g., TV Girl).

**Movies:** Films increasingly target existing online subcultures rather than seeking a general audience. In an attention-scarce environment, a guaranteed niche audience that identifies with the work is more valuable than marginal broad appeal. Examples: casting internet personalities (Stavros Halkias in *Bugonia*) to generate immediate buzz in existing communities; adapting 4chan greentext memes (The Backrooms); adopting the high-stress, frenetic visual pacing of social feeds (*Anora*, *Marty Supreme*) to prevent phone-checking.

**Language:** Slang now originates on the internet rather than merely spreading through it. Right-wing meme language has displaced African-American Vernacular English as the most dynamic source of linguistic innovation. Notably, even the vocabulary people use to critique internet influence ("extremely online," "touch grass") is itself internet-derived — revealing that those speakers have already been colonized by internet vernacular.

## Politics Is Downstream of Internet Discourse

Meme-language fluency has become a marker of political participation: J.D. Vance warning against "blackpilling," Tim Walz referencing "the couch." Politicians now communicate through "Rapid Response" accounts and internet-native press offices rather than traditional press briefings.

More consequentially, politicians and their staffers are forming their underlying views in an online milieu — paying attention to different issues, following different influencers, being socialized in a different cultural sphere. If Trump was the first online President (viral personality), Vance is the first online VP in the sense of being a habitual blogosphere content consumer whose worldview was shaped by reading Mises Wire and economic blogs.

## Traditional Media's Surviving Roles

The legacy media retains an edge in three narrow areas:

1. **Leaks.** Institutional media branding signals a reliable megaphone for important figures who need to disseminate information anonymously. This requires accumulated personal networks and differentiated public persona — a random Twitter account can't easily substitute. Leaking is more "capital intensive" than firsthand reporting.

2. **Coordination / focal points.** Traditional media can issue informal orders at pivotal moments, helping decentralized political actors coalesce around a single position. Example: legacy media's synchronized coverage helped usher Joe Biden off the 2024 ticket post-debate. The decentralized online ecosystem enables dissent to surface, but is weaker at issuing coordinated commands.

3. **Normalization / common knowledge.** The creation of "official knowledge" depends on [common knowledge](https://stevenpinker.com/publications/when-everyone-knows-everyone-knows-common-knowledge-and-mysteries-money-power-and) — not just that everyone knows Fact X, but that everyone knows *everyone else* knows it. Legacy media maintains this edge even as the online ecosystem individualizes experience. Example: anime is more widespread among Gen-Z than the NFL, but retains a "niche" aura because traditional media normalizes the NFL while ignoring anime.

## Technology as Mediating Layer (Philosophical Frame)

Torenberg draws an analogy to earlier mediating technologies: domesticating the horse extended the human range; agriculture mediates food; mathematics models the world in abstract; governments scale cooperation; currencies enable trade. None of these are "natural" but all are now constitutive of human experience.

The internet is the next such layer. The critical move is to hold two thoughts simultaneously:
- The new paradigm *is* real life — it must be accounted for, not dismissed as new or fake.
- The new paradigm is potentially dangerous — humans haven't had time to evolve alongside it, ascertain its implications, develop resistance, or reach equilibrium.

The correct response: the only way to understand the filter that online media puts on base reality is to observe the source directly. Being intentionally, critically online is how you demystify it — "the most online media consumer can be the most critical media consumer."
