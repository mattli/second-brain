# Museum Design & Curation

> TLDR: The tension between architectural spectacle and functional exhibition space defines contemporary museum-building; meanwhile, institutions are rethinking collection display from encyclopedic chronology toward thematic, cross-cultural "flow" — with mixed results.

## Architecture as Strategy

Major museums are increasingly commissioning signature buildings as competitive weapons — not just for prestige but to attract patrons, press, and a new generation of collectors. Holland Cotter's review of LACMA's new David Geffen Galleries (2026) frames this explicitly: "on the principle that design is destiny, you focus on packaging." A drop-dead building draws attention away from collection gaps and positions the institution as a "suddenly sexy" destination.

Peter Zumthor's LACMA building illustrates both the upside and the trap. Conceived as a horizontal biomorph of glass and concrete raised on piers — one story, no front or back, all undulating curves — it is genuinely stunning as architecture. A wraparound interior corridor with floor-to-ceiling windows offers changing views of the campus, city, and hills. But the same design creates dozens of dark, boxy interior galleries with immovable walls and fixed dimensions. The building cannot be structurally altered; exhibition strategy must work within its eccentricities.

The risk: when architecture becomes the primary draw, it can work against the art inside. Some display choices are undercut by the setting — South Asian sandstone carvings read as rust stains against Brutalist grey concrete. Other installations succeed precisely because they work with the building's theatrical qualities (a Qing dynasty robe, alone in a darkened space, "seems to be emerging, animate and shining, from a just-opened tomb").

## Encyclopedic vs. Global Museum

LACMA's new building operationalizes a deliberate shift in institutional identity — from "encyclopedic museum" (objects from all over the world, organized by chronology, place, and culture of origin) to "global museum" (art as a mingling of objects and influences ever on the move).

The practical expression: LACMA has organized its permanent collection display not by continents but by oceans — Atlantic, Pacific, Indian Ocean, Mediterranean Sea — which "flow and feed into each other." Visitors are explicitly told not to look for directional signage and to "let your curiosity be your compass." The concept of *flow* is embedded both in Zumthor's fluid form and in the curatorial logic it houses.

This is not novel — London's Tate Gallery experimented with a nonchronological, multicultural approach decades ago and got "seriously huffy" reactions. The LACMA approach acknowledges that an encyclopedic ambition is harder to sustain when the collection has significant gaps (thin in African and Oceanic material, spotty in Western European painting) while having notable strengths in Asian, Islamic, and South American art. A "global museum" framing lets LACMA compete on different terms than the Met.

The trade-off: wayfinding suffers. Without clear directional logic, visits can feel less like "a trip through a treasury than through a catacomb." Cotter suggests a single starter gallery that maps out several suggested exhibition routes — an AllTrails-style orientation — as a practical fix that doesn't require structural changes.

## Thematic vs. Chronological Display

Within the new building, LACMA mixes approaches:

- **Thematic installations** built around single charismatic objects tend to work well. The Ardabil carpet "center stage in a gallery lined with Persian manuscript paintings" succeeds; displays built around single masterworks like La Tour's *Magdalen With the Smoking Flame* are "winners."
- **Concept-heavy interior groupings** with multiple decorative-arts objects can feel "dryly didactic, as much about label-reading as looking."
- **Periodic rotation** is planned as part of the curatorial strategy — objects and themes will change on an unannounced timetable, a model with positive precedent at MoMA.

## Institutional Identity and the "Glocal" Museum

LACMA's most distinctive competitive claim is its position as an explicitly Los Angeles institution that reflects the multicultural city with "a vital Latino overlay." Cotter argues this is what gives LACMA its "defining edge." The inaugural display leads with a sequence no other large American museum could replicate: ancient West Mexican spirit-dog sculpture, Rivera's *Flower Day* (first work by Rivera to enter an American museum collection), Chicano activist poster art (Yolanda M. López's 1978 "Who's the illegal alien, PILGRIM?"), and Liz Glynn's 2023 sculpture *The Futility of Conquest* — all in proximity.

This "glocal" framing — simultaneously global and local — is the institution's bet for differentiation against encyclopedic giants like the Met. Whether the new building's architectural spectacle serves or undermines this mission is the open question as the collection grows.
